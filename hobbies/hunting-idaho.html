Hunting in Idaho 🦌

Elk, deer, antelope, upland birds, and waterfowl in one of the best big-game states in the lower 48 — seasons, units, draws, and how to actually get started

Why Idaho for Hunting

Idaho is one of the best big-game hunting states in the United States. The combination of huge tracts of public land (about 70% of the state), healthy populations of elk, mule deer, white-tailed deer, antelope, black bear, and mountain lion, plus reasonable nonresident pricing and over-the-counter (OTC) tag availability make it a destination for hunters from across the country. For residents, the value is even better — hunting in Idaho is one of the best deals in American outdoor recreation.

The state has more than 30 million acres of public land — more than New York, Pennsylvania, and Ohio combined. Most of it is huntable. And unlike some western states where every elk hunt requires drawing a tag through a lottery years in advance, Idaho still offers legitimate over-the-counter elk and deer tags in many units.

This guide is the orientation for someone new to Idaho hunting or new to hunting entirely. It's not a how-to-shoot-your-first-elk guide — that would take a book. It's the framework: what species, what tags, what seasons, what units, and how to plug into the hunt.

The Idaho Big Game Species

Elk — The Crown Jewel

Elk are why people come to Idaho. The state has one of the largest elk populations in the lower 48 (estimated 100,000+ animals), and unlike Montana or Wyoming, Idaho still offers OTC tags in many premier units. The downside is that "OTC" means you're competing with everyone else holding the same tag — backcountry units like Lolo and Selway can have heavy hunter pressure during rifle season.

Elk hunting is hard. They live in steep, remote country. They cover huge distances. They have excellent senses. The standard joke is that elk hunting is 90% hiking and 10% shooting — and the 10% only happens if you've done the 90% right. Be prepared for multi-day backcountry hunts, miles of off-trail terrain, and the possibility of going home with nothing.

Mule deer are the classic western big game animal — large, beautiful, distinctive ears, found in high-desert and mountain habitats throughout Idaho. They're generally easier to hunt than elk (smaller home range, more visible) but a mature 4×4 mule deer buck is a real trophy.

White-Tailed Deer

DetailInfo
SeasonsSame as mule deer (Idaho doesn't differentiate the season)
Tag typesSame general deer tag
WherePrimarily northern Idaho — the panhandle, around Coeur d'Alene, Sandpoint, Lewiston

Whitetails dominate northern Idaho's lower elevations. Hunting them is more like Midwestern whitetail hunting than western mountain hunting — stand hunting near agricultural areas, river bottoms, and timber edges.

Antelope (Pronghorn)

DetailInfo
SeasonsMostly August (archery) and September (rifle)
Tag availabilityDraw only — not OTC
Best unitsSouth-central Idaho — Owyhee, Big Desert, Mountain Home area

Pronghorn antelope live in Idaho's high desert. Tags are draw-only, so plan ahead — apply each May for the fall hunt. Once drawn, they're one of the easier western big game hunts because pronghorn live in open country and are highly visible. Spot-and-stalk on flat terrain.

Black Bear & Mountain Lion

Idaho has healthy populations of both. Black bear has spring and fall seasons in many units (the spring hunt is unique to Idaho and the Pacific Northwest). Mountain lion is hunted with hounds in winter — a specialized, guided endeavor. Both are OTC in many units. Bears require additional tags beyond the general license.

Wolves

Idaho's wolf population is huntable. Tags are inexpensive ($11 resident) and seasons are long. Wolf hunting is controversial nationally but a normal part of Idaho's wildlife management.

Moose & Mountain Goat

Both species exist in Idaho but are draw-only with extremely low odds. Most hunters apply for years before drawing. Worth applying if you live here — it's basically a lottery ticket — but don't plan on a moose hunt as a near-term goal.

Tag System Basics

Idaho's tag system is friendlier than many western states but still confusing for new hunters. Here's the breakdown:

The Hunting License

Every hunter needs a base hunting license. Resident annual is $31, nonresident is $185. This is required even if you're only hunting one species. Buy it first.

Species-Specific Tags

On top of the license, you need a tag for each animal you intend to hunt. Tags are species-specific (elk, deer, antelope, bear) and often unit-specific. Tag costs vary:

  • Resident elk tag: $36
  • Resident deer tag: $24
  • Resident bear tag: $11
  • Nonresident elk tag: $651
  • Nonresident deer tag: $352

OTC vs Controlled Hunts (Draws)

Idaho's tag system has two flavors:

  • General season / OTC (over-the-counter) — Tags you can buy directly from a Fish & Game office or website without applying. Available in many units, especially for elk and deer.
  • Controlled hunts (draws) — Limited-quota tags awarded by lottery. Apply by mid-May for the upcoming fall season. Better areas and better odds, but you might not draw.

The Capped vs Uncapped Distinction

Idaho elk tags are split into two categories that confuse new hunters:

  • Capped zones — Limited number of tags issued. First-come, first-served sale on Dec 1. Popular zones (Sawtooth, Boise) sell out quickly.
  • Uncapped zones — No tag limit, tags available all year. Less pressure to act on Dec 1.

This system changes periodically. Always check the current Idaho Big Game Rules booklet at idfg.idaho.gov before planning a hunt.

The Idaho Game Management Units

Idaho is divided into about 100 Game Management Units (GMUs) for big-game purposes. Each unit has its own rules, season dates, tag types, and quality. The unit system is critical to understand:

  • Unit rules vary. A unit might allow either-sex elk in archery but only spike elk in rifle. Or antlerless deer might be allowed in some units but not others. Always read the regulations for the specific unit you're hunting.
  • Quality varies dramatically. Some units are famous trophy areas with low hunter density. Others are roadside hunts with heavy pressure.
  • Access varies. Some units are mostly public land (Selway, Lolo); others are mostly private (parts of southern Idaho).

How to Actually Get Started Hunting

Hunting is one of the harder hobbies to enter cold. The barriers — gear, knowledge, ethics, regulations, physical demands — combine to discourage many would-be hunters. Here's the realistic progression for someone starting from zero:

Step 1: Take Hunter Education

Required by Idaho law for anyone born after January 1, 1975 to purchase a hunting license. Free or cheap, available online or in-person. Sign up at idfg.idaho.gov. Complete it before doing anything else.

Step 2: Find a Mentor

Hunting is hard to learn from books and YouTube. The best path is to find someone who already hunts and ask to tag along — to scout, to glass, to help pack out an animal someone else shoots, to learn how the day actually unfolds. Idaho has a strong hunting culture; most experienced hunters are willing to help beginners.

If you don't know any hunters personally, look at:

  • Idaho Fish & Game Hunter Mentorship Program — Sometimes pairs new hunters with experienced ones
  • Backcountry Hunters & Anglers (BHA) — National conservation org with active Idaho chapter, organizes events and mentor opportunities
  • Local rod & gun clubs — The Boise area has multiple, most welcome new members
  • Idaho Wildlife Federation — Statewide conservation org

Step 3: Buy the Right Gear (Don't Overspend)

Hunting gear marketing is endless. The reality is that a successful hunt requires surprisingly little. The first-year essentials:

  • Rifle — A bolt-action .270, .308, .30-06, or 6.5 Creedmoor will kill anything in Idaho. Used Tikka T3, Ruger American, or Savage Axis are great budget options ($400–$700). Don't buy a $3,000 custom rifle for your first hunt.
  • Optics — A 3-9x or 4-12x variable scope. Vortex Diamondback or Crossfire are excellent value ($150–$300). Spend more here than on the rifle.
  • Binoculars — 8x42 or 10x42. Vortex Viper, Maven C1, or Nikon Monarch are mid-range standards ($300–$500). Critical for spot-and-stalk hunting.
  • Boots — The single most important comfort item. Get fitted at a real outdoor store. Crispi, Lowa, Kenetrek, or Meindl. $250–$500. Don't skimp.
  • Pack — A 4,000–5,500 cubic inch hunting pack. Mystery Ranch, Stone Glacier, Eberlestock, or Kifaru. $250–$700.
  • Layering system — Merino base layers, fleece mid layer, waterproof shell. First Lite, Sitka, Kuiu (premium); Cabela's or REI brands (budget).
  • Knife — A fixed blade hunting knife — Buck, Benchmade Hidden Canyon, or any solid 3.5–4 inch blade
  • Game bags — Lightweight breathable bags for carrying meat out of the field

Total realistic first-year setup: $1,500–$2,500 if you're shopping smart. Yes, that's a lot. No, you don't need to spend more.

Step 4: Practice Shooting

You owe it to the animal to make a clean shot. Practice from field positions (not just bench rest), at varying distances (50, 100, 200, 300 yards), in different conditions (wind, light). The Boise area has multiple outdoor shooting ranges. Shoot at least 100 rounds before your first hunt.

Step 5: Scout the Area

Don't show up to your hunting unit on opening day having never seen it. Drive in during summer. Hike potential glassing areas. Look for sign (tracks, scat, rubs, beds, food sources, water sources). Use OnX Hunt or Gaia GPS to mark spots and track public/private boundaries. Scouting trips are 50% of hunting success.

Step 6: Plan a Realistic First Hunt

Don't try to kill a 350-class bull elk in the Lolo Zone for your first hunt. Try this instead: an antlerless deer hunt in a unit close to home, during muzzleloader or rifle season (slower pace than archery), with someone experienced if possible. The goal is to experience the process — scouting, packing in, glassing, shooting, field dressing, packing out. Killing an animal is the point, but learning the process matters more on the first hunt.

Step 7: Pack Out the Meat

If you kill an animal, you're committed to packing the meat out, processing it, and using it. This is the hardest physical part of hunting and the most important ethically. A bull elk produces 200+ pounds of boned-out meat that has to come off the mountain on your back (and your friends' backs). Plan for it.

The Hunting Year in Idaho

January–March: Off-season

Plan next year. Apply for controlled hunts when applications open. Maintain gear. Practice shooting at indoor ranges.

April–May: Spring Bear & Apply

Spring black bear season is one of Idaho's best-kept hunting secrets. Less crowded than fall, more aggressive bears, rewarding hunt. Also: controlled hunt application deadline is in late May. Don't miss it.

June–July: Scout

The best months for scouting. Roads are open, weather is good, animals are visible at higher elevations. Take a long weekend, drive into your unit, hike, glass, mark spots. This work pays off in October.

August: Archery Season Opens

Late August opens elk archery season. Cool mornings, hot afternoons, animals still in summer patterns. Bow hunters love this month for the early elk activity before the rut.

September: The Rut

Mid-September is the elk rut — bulls are bugling, vocal, distracted by cows, more vulnerable. Most successful archery elk hunts happen in September. The best month for elk hunting in many units.

October: Rifle Season

The default Idaho hunting month. Rifle seasons open in most units. Cooler weather, animals moving down to winter range, the highest hunter density of the year. Plan for crowds in popular units.

November: Late Season

Some units have late rifle seasons that extend into November. Snow at higher elevations pushes animals down. Cold, sometimes brutal, but quality if you can handle it.

December: The End

Last few open units, mostly bird hunting and lion hunting now. Time to start planning the next year.

Birds & Waterfowl (Not Just Big Game)

Big game gets the attention but Idaho also has excellent bird hunting:

Upland Birds

  • Chukar — Idaho's defining upland bird. Lives in steep rocky terrain. Hunting chukar is the hardest, most addictive bird hunting in America. The Owyhees and Snake River canyons are prime country.
  • Hungarian Partridge (Huns) — Smaller, easier-to-find than chukar. Found in CRP fields and grasslands.
  • Pheasant — Heavily stocked in southern Idaho's wildlife management areas. Easier hunting, family-friendly.
  • Forest Grouse (blue, ruffed, spruce) — Found in mountain timber. "Fool hens" — easy hunting compared to upland birds.
  • Quail — Limited but present in southwestern Idaho

Waterfowl

  • Mallards, pintails, wigeon, teal — Migratory ducks pass through Idaho in fall. The Snake River, Boise River, and reservoirs are popular hunting waters.
  • Geese — Snake River and Treasure Valley agricultural fields hold large goose populations
  • Pacific Flyway timing — October and November are peak migration months

Bird hunting is generally more accessible than big game — shorter days, easier physical demands, more accessible to beginners, often family-friendly. If big game feels overwhelming, start with chukar or pheasant hunting.

The Three Hunts Every Idaho Hunter Should Try

  1. A general-season antlerless deer hunt close to home — Boise River Zone or Owyhees. The "starter" hunt that teaches you everything without the pressure of finding a trophy bull.
  2. An archery elk hunt during the September rut — One of the great American hunting experiences. Bugling bulls, cool mornings, intimate hunting at close range. The reason many hunters say they hunt.
  3. A backcountry elk or mule deer trip in a wilderness unit — Multi-day, pack-in, remote. The Selway, Frank Church, or Owyhee Wilderness. Once-in-a-lifetime country, even if you don't kill anything.

Final Thoughts

Idaho is one of the best places in America to hunt, and one of the best states for someone learning to hunt. The opportunities are real, the public land is enormous, and the cost is manageable for residents. The hardest part isn't the regulations or the gear — it's finding a mentor and committing to the time it takes to learn.

Start with hunter education. Find someone to learn from. Buy gear that's good enough rather than perfect. Scout in summer. Hunt in fall. Pack the meat out. Eat it through the winter. Repeat.

This is the most rewarding hobby on this site for someone who wants a deep, hands-on connection with the wild country and the food they eat. It's also the hardest. The two go together.

Public land. Wild meat. Earn it.
