1932 Ford Buyers Guide — Flathead V8 / 5-Speed Hot Rod 🔧

A guide for finding and buying a 1932 Ford with a flathead V8 and 5-speed transmission

Why the '32 Ford?

The 1932 Ford is the hot rod. It's where the hot rod movement started — kids in the '40s and '50s pulled fenders off these things and dropped hopped-up flatheads in them. It's the most iconic hot rod platform ever made, and it's still the gold standard today.

Most Desirable (and Expensive) Body Styles

  1. Roadster (Deuce Roadster) — The holy grail. Open-top, two-seat. $60K–$200K+ depending on build quality and originality
  2. 3-Window Coupe — The classic hot rod look. $45K–$150K+
  3. 5-Window Coupe — Slightly more common, still iconic. $35K–$120K+
  4. Tudor Sedan — More affordable entry point. $25K–$80K
  5. Pickup — Cool factor is high. $30K–$90K
  6. Phaeton — Open 4-seat. Rare, beautiful. $50K–$150K+

Common Hop-Up Specs

Most '32 Ford hot rods you'll find for sale have been built up. Common upgrades:

UpgradeWhat It Does
Bigger displacement239ci (1946–48) or 255ci (Mercury, 1949–53) blocks are popular swaps. Same external dimensions.
Edelbrock/Offenhauser intakeDual-carb or tri-power setups. Huge aftermarket even today.
Isky/Winfield camHotter cam profiles for more power
HeadersHuge improvement over stock exhaust manifolds. Zoomie headers for the classic look.
Ported/relieved headsOpens up the combustion chambers
High-compression headsEdelbrock, Navarro, or modern aftermarket aluminum heads
Realistic output100–180 hp with typical street build. 200+ for serious builds.

What to Watch For

  • Overheating — The #1 flathead problem. The exhaust ports run through the block next to the intake. Get a good cooling system (modern aluminum radiator, electric fan).
  • Cracked blocks — Common, especially around the exhaust passages. Inspect carefully.
  • Oil leaks — They all leak. It's character. But excessive leaking means worn seals/gaskets.
  • Parts availability — Excellent. The flathead aftermarket is still thriving. Speedway Motors, Honest Charley, H&H Flatheads, etc.
  • Rebuilds — A quality flathead rebuild runs $3,000–$8,000 depending on level.

Flathead vs. Modern V8 Swap

Many '32 Fords on the market have small-block Chevys or Ford 302s/351s. If you want a flathead specifically, you're looking at a smaller but passionate market. Flathead builds are more authentic and have a sound/character that modern engines can't replicate — that lopey idle and exhaust rumble is unmistakable.

The 5-Speed Transmission

A 5-speed in a '32 Ford is an aftermarket upgrade (the originals had 3-speed unsynchronized boxes). Here's what you'll typically find:

Common 5-Speed Options

TransmissionSourceNotes
Tremec TKO 500/600AftermarketMost popular choice for hot rods. Strong, smooth shifting. 500 lb-ft torque capacity. Overdrive 5th gear. $2,500–$3,500 new.
Tremec T-5Ford Mustang (1983–2004)Lighter duty, good for stock-power flatheads. Common and affordable. $800–$1,500 used.
Ford Top Loader (4-speed)Ford (1964–73)Not a 5-speed, but worth mentioning — period-correct hot rod trans. Bulletproof.
Toyota W58/R154Toyota SupraBudget option some builders use. Strong, cheap, but requires adapter.
S10/S15 5-speedGM trucksAnother budget option. Compact.

Why 5-Speed?

  • Overdrive — Drops RPMs on the highway. A flathead at 3,500 RPM on the freeway is miserable. Overdrive brings it down to ~2,200 RPM at 65 mph.
  • Modern drivability — Synchronized gears, smooth shifts
  • Fuel economy — Matters when you're cruising to Santa Fe in your '32 😎

Adapter Kits

If the car has a flathead + 5-speed, someone used an adapter kit. Common sources:

  • Wilcap Company — Flathead-to-Tremec adapters (the go-to)
  • Speedway Motors — Various adapter plates
  • H&H Flatheads — Flathead specialists

Flathead Premium/Discount

  • Cars with flathead V8s can go either way — traditional hot rod purists pay MORE for a flathead build. SBC-swapped cars are more common.
  • A well-built flathead car in the $45K–$75K range is the sweet spot for a fun driver.
  • 5-speed equipped adds $2,000–$4,000 in value over a 3-speed or C4 auto.

What to Look For When Buying

Body & Frame

  • Steel or fiberglass body? — Ask directly. Magnet test (magnet won't stick to fiberglass). Steel is worth more.
  • Reproduction body? — Many "steel" bodies are actually new Brookville or Dearborn Deuce reproductions (which are excellent quality but should be priced accordingly)
  • Original Henry Ford steel? — The real deal. VIN on the frame. Worth significantly more.
  • Frame — Original rails, aftermarket (TCI, Chassis Engineering, Pete & Jakes, SO-CAL), or custom? Aftermarket is fine — often better.
  • Rust — Check floors, firewall, lower quarters, trunk area. '32s are 90+ years old. Some rust is expected.
  • Body filler — Magnet test or paint thickness gauge. Some is fine, tons is a red flag.
  • Gaps and alignment — Doors, hood, trunk lid should fit evenly.

Engine (Flathead V8)

  • Which flathead? — 221ci (1932–38), 239ci (1939–48), 255ci (Mercury 49–53). Ask for specifics.
  • Rebuild history — When was it last rebuilt? By whom? Receipts/documentation?
  • Compression test — Should be 90–120 PSI per cylinder, even across all 8
  • Oil pressure — Should be 30–45 PSI warm at idle
  • Cooling system — Modern aluminum radiator? Electric fan? Overheating history?
  • Exhaust leaks — Listen carefully. Flatheads develop cracks.
  • Speed equipment — What heads, cam, intake, carbs? Edelbrock and Offenhauser equipment is desirable.

Transmission (5-Speed)

  • Which 5-speed? — Tremec TKO is ideal. T-5 is okay for stock power.
  • Adapter kit — Wilcap or other quality adapter?
  • Clutch — Hydraulic throwout bearing is ideal (no clutch linkage headaches)
  • Shifts smoothly — Drive it through all gears. No grinding, popping out of gear.
  • Overdrive works — Get it on the highway and confirm 5th gear drops RPMs

Brakes, Suspension, Steering

  • Brakes — Disc fronts are a must for safety. Disc rears are nice. Drum-only is sketchy for modern driving.
  • Steering — Vega box, rack-and-pinion, or cross-steer? Should be tight, no wandering.
  • Front suspension — Dropped axle (traditional) or independent? Both are fine. IFS is smoother.
  • Rear end — Ford 9-inch, 8-inch, or early Ford banjo? 9-inch is best.

Electrical

  • 12-volt conversion? — Should be. 6-volt is a hassle.
  • Wiring quality — Spaghetti wiring = fire hazard. Look for quality harness (Painless, American Autowire).
  • Turn signals, brake lights, headlights — All working? You need them for road driving.

Documentation & Title

  • Clean title — Some states title these as "assembled vehicles" or "replica." Know your state's rules (Idaho is pretty friendly for hot rods).
  • Build documentation — Photos, receipts, builder info. Adds value and trust.
  • History — How many owners? Show history? Magazine features?

Idaho-Specific Tips

Registration

  • Idaho is very hot rod friendly. No emissions testing. No safety inspections.
  • Antique plates available (1932 qualifies easily) — reduced registration fees
  • Idaho also offers custom vehicle plates

Insurance

  • Hagerty — The go-to for collector car insurance. Agreed-value policies. Affordable ($300–$800/year typically).
  • Grundy — Another good option
  • American Collectors — Good rates

Driving a '32 Ford in Idaho

  • Perfect cruise weather June–September
  • Star → Horseshoe Bend → Garden Valley is an incredible hot rod cruise (winding mountain road, river views)
  • Boise cruise nights and car shows happen regularly in summer
  • Idaho to Bonneville Speed Week (August, Wendover, UT) is a classic road trip — 3.5 hours

Suggested Search Strategy

  1. Set up alerts on Bring a Trailer, Hemmings, and ClassicCars.com for "1932 Ford"
  2. Join The H.A.M.B. (jalopyjournal.com) — browse the classifieds weekly. This is where the flathead guys sell to each other.
  3. Join Facebook groups: "1932 Ford Deuce Coupe," "Flathead V8 Buy Sell Trade," "Traditional Hot Rods for Sale"
  4. Be patient — The right flathead/5-speed '32 is specific. It might take a few months to find the right one.
  5. Be ready to travel — SoCal, Texas, and the Midwest have the most inventory. Budget for a one-way plane ticket + driving it home (which is half the fun).
  6. Budget — For a nice flathead/5-speed driver: target $45,000–$75,000. You can find deals below that, and you can spend way more, but that's the sweet spot for a well-built cruiser.
