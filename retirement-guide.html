1. Know Your Number

The 4% Rule (and Why It‚Äôs a Starting Point)

The most widely cited retirement rule of thumb is the ‚Äú4% rule,‚Äù based on the 1994 Trinity Study. The idea: if you withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year one and adjust for inflation each year after, your money has historically lasted 30 years about 95% of the time. To find your target number, take your expected annual spending and multiply by 25. Spending $80,000/year? You need $2,000,000. Spending $120,000? You need $3,000,000.

But retiring at 60 means you may need 30‚Äì35+ years of income, which stretches the original study‚Äôs assumptions. Many financial planners recommend a more conservative 3.5% withdrawal rate for early retirees, which means multiplying annual spending by ~28‚Äì30x instead of 25x. At $80K/year spending, that bumps your target to $2.3‚Äì2.4M.

Spending Scenarios

Think about which lifestyle tier fits you:

  • Lean FIRE ($40,000/year): Minimal, frugal lifestyle. Need ~$1.0‚Äì1.2M. Tight but doable ‚Äî requires low housing costs (paid-off home or cheap area), minimal travel, and good health.
  • Comfortable FIRE ($80,000/year): Covers a solid middle-class lifestyle with travel, hobbies, dining out, and a buffer. Need ~$2.0‚Äì2.4M. This is the sweet spot most people target.
  • Fat FIRE ($120,000+/year): Premium lifestyle ‚Äî nicer travel, new cars, generous gifting, luxury hobbies. Need $3.0‚Äì3.6M+.

Monte Carlo Simulations

Don‚Äôt rely solely on the 4% rule. Use Monte Carlo simulation tools (available free at FIRECalc.com, cFIREsim, or through advisors) that run your specific scenario through thousands of possible market histories. They give you a probability of success ‚Äî aim for 90%+ across 35 years. These tools let you model variable spending, Social Security start dates, pensions, and one-time expenses like a new roof or car.

Adjusting for Inflation

A dollar today won‚Äôt buy a dollar‚Äôs worth of goods in 20 years. At 3% average inflation, $80K in today‚Äôs dollars becomes ~$144K in 20 years. Your investments need to outpace inflation, which is why keeping a significant equity allocation matters even in retirement. When calculating your number, use today‚Äôs dollars for simplicity but make sure your withdrawal strategy adjusts for inflation annually.

2. Bridge the Gap (60‚Äì67)

The Age Milestones You Must Know

Retiring at 60 means navigating a minefield of age-gated benefits:

Age Milestone
59¬Ω Penalty-free withdrawals from 401(k) and traditional IRA
62 Earliest Social Security (but permanently reduced ~30% vs.¬†full retirement age)
65 Medicare eligibility
67 Full Social Security retirement age (for those born 1960+)
70 Maximum Social Security benefit (delayed credits stop)
73 Required Minimum Distributions begin (SECURE 2.0)

At 60, you‚Äôre past 59¬Ω so you can access retirement accounts penalty-free. But you‚Äôre still 2 years from the earliest Social Security and 5 years from Medicare. This 60‚Äì65 window is the most expensive and complex bridge to plan for.

Rule of 55 and 72(t) SEPP

If you leave your employer at age 55 or later, the Rule of 55 lets you withdraw from that specific employer‚Äôs 401(k) penalty-free (not IRAs, not old 401(k)s ‚Äî only the plan of the employer you separated from). This is huge if you retire between 55‚Äì59¬Ω.

If you need IRA money before 59¬Ω, 72(t) Substantially Equal Periodic Payments (SEPP) allow penalty-free withdrawals based on your life expectancy. But beware: once you start, you must continue for 5 years or until 59¬Ω (whichever is later), and if you modify the payments, you owe all the penalties retroactively. It‚Äôs rigid but useful in a pinch.

The Roth Contribution Ladder

This is the early retiree‚Äôs secret weapon. You can withdraw Roth IRA contributions (not earnings) at any time, any age, tax- and penalty-free. If you‚Äôve been contributing for years, you may have a nice pool of contributions to draw from. Additionally, Roth conversions become accessible after a 5-year seasoning period ‚Äî so if you start converting at 55, that money is available at 60.

How Much to Keep Liquid

Keep 2‚Äì5 years of living expenses in easily accessible accounts: high-yield savings, money market funds, short-term bond funds, or a taxable brokerage account. This serves two purposes: (1) it covers the bridge to Social Security and Medicare, and (2) it protects you from selling stocks during a downturn in early retirement (sequence of returns risk). At $80K/year spending, that‚Äôs $160K‚Äì$400K in accessible, low-volatility assets.

3. Healthcare Plan

ACA Marketplace (Your Best Option at 60)

For most early retirees, the Affordable Care Act (ACA) marketplace is the go-to for health insurance from 60 to 65. Premium subsidies are based on your Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI). In 2025, a household of two earning ~$40,000‚Äì$60,000 MAGI can qualify for significant subsidies that bring Silver plan premiums down to $200‚Äì$500/month. The key strategy: control your MAGI through careful Roth conversions and capital gains harvesting to stay in the subsidy sweet spot.

The enhanced ACA subsidies (no cliff, capped at 8.5% of income) were extended through 2025 and may be extended further. Without subsidies, a 60-year-old couple can face premiums of $1,500‚Äì$2,500/month for a Silver plan. Managing your taxable income is literally worth thousands per month.

COBRA and Other Options

COBRA lets you keep your employer‚Äôs health plan for up to 18 months after leaving, but you pay the full premium (employer + employee share) plus a 2% admin fee. This typically runs $600‚Äì$800/month for an individual or $1,500‚Äì$2,000+ for a family. It‚Äôs expensive but can be useful for a short bridge ‚Äî especially if you have ongoing treatment with specific providers.

Health sharing ministries (Medi-Share, Christian Healthcare Ministries, etc.) are cheaper ($200‚Äì$500/month) but are not insurance. They don‚Äôt have to cover pre-existing conditions, can deny claims, and aren‚Äôt regulated like insurance. Use with extreme caution.

Dental, Vision, and Long-Term Care

Medicare doesn‚Äôt cover dental or vision (mostly). Budget $50‚Äì$150/month for supplemental dental/vision coverage, or self-insure with savings. Dental costs can spike ‚Äî a single crown runs $1,000‚Äì$2,000, implants $3,000‚Äì$5,000.

Long-term care (LTC) insurance is worth considering in your mid-to-late 50s. The average cost of a nursing home is $95,000‚Äì$110,000/year; assisted living runs $55,000‚Äì$65,000/year. LTC premiums get dramatically more expensive with age and health issues. A couple buying policies at 55‚Äì60 might pay $2,000‚Äì$5,000/year combined. Hybrid life/LTC policies are increasingly popular as they guarantee some benefit even if you never need care.

Mega Backdoor Roth

If your employer‚Äôs 401(k) plan allows after-tax contributions and in-service distributions/rollovers, you can contribute up to the total 415(c) limit ($70,000 in 2025, including employer match) and convert the after-tax portion to Roth. This can add $30,000‚Äì$40,000+ per year to your Roth balance. Check with your plan administrator ‚Äî not all plans allow this, but if yours does, it‚Äôs the single most powerful savings accelerator available.

Employer Match and Spousal IRA

Never leave free money on the table. If your employer matches 401(k) contributions, that‚Äôs an instant 50‚Äì100% return. At minimum, contribute enough to get the full match.

If you have a non-working or lower-earning spouse, a spousal IRA lets them contribute up to $7,000 + $1,000 catch-up ($8,000 total) even with no earned income, as long as the working spouse has enough earned income to cover both contributions. That‚Äôs an extra $8,000/year in tax-advantaged savings.

HSA: The Triple-Tax Advantage

The Health Savings Account is the most tax-efficient account in existence: contributions are tax-deductible, growth is tax-free, and withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. After age 65, you can withdraw for any purpose (taxed as income, like a traditional IRA, but no penalty). Strategy: pay medical expenses out of pocket now, save receipts, and let the HSA grow. Withdraw decades later tax-free for those old receipts.

5. Eliminate Debt Before 60

The Mortgage Payoff Debate

This is the most debated topic in retirement planning. The math says: if your mortgage rate is 3‚Äì4% and the stock market averages 7‚Äì10%, you‚Äôre better off investing. But retirement isn‚Äôt just math ‚Äî it‚Äôs psychology. A paid-off home means:

  • Your baseline monthly expenses drop dramatically ($1,500‚Äì$3,000/month savings is common)
  • You need a smaller portfolio (lower withdrawal rate)
  • You have more flexibility if markets crash
  • Peace of mind is worth something

If your mortgage rate is above 5‚Äì6%, paying it off is almost always the right call. Below 4%, it‚Äôs a personal decision. One middle ground: pay off the mortgage the year you retire, using a lump sum from taxable accounts.

Which Debts to Kill First

Use the debt avalanche method (highest interest rate first) for maximum savings, or the debt snowball (smallest balance first) for psychological momentum. In practice:

  1. Credit cards (15‚Äì25% APR) ‚Äî eliminate immediately, no debate
  2. Car loans (5‚Äì8%) ‚Äî pay off before retirement
  3. Student loans (4‚Äì7%) ‚Äî pay off or ensure manageable payments
  4. Mortgage (3‚Äì7%) ‚Äî strategic decision (see above)

Impact on Withdrawal Rates

Going into retirement debt-free can reduce your required annual spending by 20‚Äì40%. If your mortgage payment is $2,000/month ($24,000/year), eliminating it means you need $24,000 less from your portfolio annually. Using the 4% rule, that‚Äôs $600,000 less you need saved. Debt-free retirement is the single biggest lever most people have to make early retirement viable.

6. Diversify Income Streams

Social Security Optimization

Social Security is likely your largest single retirement asset. Key strategies:

  • Delay if possible: Each year you delay past 62 increases your benefit by ~6.7%/year until 67 (full retirement age), then 8%/year until 70. A $2,000/month benefit at 62 becomes ~$2,860 at 67 and ~$3,550 at 70 ‚Äî a 77% increase.
  • Breakeven analysis: Delaying from 62 to 67 breaks even around age 78‚Äì80. Delaying to 70 breaks even around 80‚Äì82. If you expect to live past 85, delaying is almost always worth it.
  • Spousal benefits: A lower-earning spouse can claim up to 50% of the higher earner‚Äôs benefit at full retirement age. Coordinate claiming strategies as a couple.
  • Survivor benefits: The surviving spouse gets the higher of the two benefits. This is a huge reason for the higher earner to delay ‚Äî it protects the surviving spouse.

Dividend and Investment Income

Building a portfolio that generates $20,000‚Äì$40,000/year in dividends and interest can significantly reduce the need to sell shares. A diversified portfolio of dividend-paying stocks and bond funds yielding 2.5‚Äì3.5% on $1M generates $25,000‚Äì$35,000/year. Focus on dividend growth stocks (companies that raise dividends annually) rather than chasing high yields, which often signal risk.

Part-Time Work and Rental Income

Even modest part-time income of $15,000‚Äì$25,000/year in early retirement has an outsized impact. Research shows that earning $20K/year for the first 5 years of retirement can improve portfolio survival by 10‚Äì15 percentage points. Consulting, freelancing, or part-time work you enjoy can bridge the gap and keep you socially engaged.

Rental income can be excellent passive income but requires capital, management effort, and carries risk (vacancies, repairs, bad tenants). If you already own rental property, great. Buying investment property specifically for retirement income requires careful analysis of cash-on-cash returns, typically targeting 6‚Äì10% net.

Annuities: Proceed with Caution

A Single Premium Immediate Annuity (SPIA) can provide guaranteed lifetime income, essentially creating your own pension. At 60, a $200,000 SPIA might generate ~$900‚Äì$1,100/month for life. Pros: guaranteed income floor, longevity protection, peace of mind. Cons: you give up the principal, low returns vs.¬†market, limited inflation protection, and if you die early, the money may be gone. Consider annuitizing only 20‚Äì30% of your portfolio ‚Äî enough for a baseline income floor, not so much that you sacrifice growth.

7. Tax Strategy

Roth Conversion Ladder (Step by Step)

This is one of the most powerful tools for early retirees:

  1. Year 1 (age 60): You retire. Your income drops to near zero. Convert $50,000‚Äì$80,000 from traditional IRA to Roth IRA, filling up the 12% or 22% tax bracket.
  2. Pay taxes on the conversion from taxable accounts or savings ‚Äî not from the IRA itself.
  3. Years 2‚Äì5: Repeat annual conversions, staying within your target tax bracket.
  4. Year 6+: The Year 1 conversion has now ‚Äúseasoned‚Äù for 5 years and can be withdrawn from the Roth completely tax- and penalty-free.
  5. Meanwhile: Live off taxable brokerage accounts, Roth contributions, and savings during the 5-year seasoning period.

This effectively moves money from a tax-deferred account to a tax-free account at a low tax rate, saving you potentially hundreds of thousands in taxes over your lifetime.

Tax Bracket Optimization

In 2025, the federal tax brackets for married filing jointly are:

  • 10%: $0‚Äì$23,850
  • 12%: $23,851‚Äì$96,950
  • 22%: $96,951‚Äì$206,700
  • 24%: $206,701‚Äì$394,600

With the standard deduction of ~$32,300 (MFJ, 65+), a retired couple can have ~$129,250 in gross income and stay in the 22% bracket. Fill up the lower brackets each year with Roth conversions ‚Äî don‚Äôt leave cheap tax space on the table.

Capital Gains Harvesting

In 2025, married couples filing jointly pay 0% capital gains tax on up to ~$94,050 in taxable income (including long-term capital gains). In early retirement with low ordinary income, you can strategically sell appreciated investments, pay zero capital gains tax, and reset your cost basis. This is free money ‚Äî do it every year your income is low enough.

State Tax Considerations

States vary wildly. Idaho has a state income tax (5.695% flat rate as of 2023+). States with no income tax include Nevada, Wyoming, Texas, Florida, Washington, Tennessee, and South Dakota. If you‚Äôre flexible on location, moving to a no-income-tax state can save $5,000‚Äì$20,000+/year depending on your income level. Some states also exempt Social Security or pension income from state tax.

RMDs and QCDs

Under SECURE 2.0, Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) start at age 73 (75 for those born 1960+). At that point, the IRS forces you to withdraw from traditional accounts whether you need the money or not. Roth conversions in your 60s reduce future RMDs. After age 70¬Ω, Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs) let you donate up to $105,000/year directly from your IRA to charity ‚Äî it counts toward your RMD but isn‚Äôt included in taxable income. If you‚Äôre charitably inclined, this is extremely tax-efficient.

8. Plan for Longevity

Asset Allocation Glide Paths

At 60, you need your money to last 30‚Äì35+ years. Going too conservative too early is one of the biggest mistakes retirees make. A common starting allocation:

  • Age 60: 55‚Äì65% stocks / 35‚Äì45% bonds
  • Age 70: 45‚Äì55% stocks / 45‚Äì55% bonds
  • Age 80+: 35‚Äì45% stocks / 55‚Äì65% bonds

Some research (Wade Pfau, Michael Kitces) actually suggests a rising equity glide path ‚Äî starting with ~30% stocks and increasing to 60‚Äì70% over time ‚Äî because it mitigates sequence of returns risk. The key: never go below 30% stocks, even in your 80s. You need growth to beat inflation.

The Bucket Strategy

Organize your portfolio into three ‚Äúbuckets‚Äù:

  • Bucket 1 (1‚Äì2 years): Cash, high-yield savings, money market. Covers near-term expenses. You draw from here monthly. Refill from Bucket 2.
  • Bucket 2 (3‚Äì7 years): Short-to-intermediate bonds, bond funds, CDs. Provides stability and income. Refill Bucket 1 periodically.
  • Bucket 3 (8+ years): Stock index funds, growth investments. This is your long-term engine. You don‚Äôt touch this during downturns ‚Äî let it recover and grow.

This structure means you never have to sell stocks in a crash. Your 1‚Äì2 years of cash buys time for markets to recover.

Sequence of Returns Risk

The biggest threat to early retirees isn‚Äôt average returns ‚Äî it‚Äôs bad returns in the first 5 years. A 30% market drop in year 1 of retirement, combined with withdrawals, can permanently impair your portfolio even if markets recover. Mitigation strategies:

  • Keep 2+ years in cash/bonds (bucket strategy)
  • Reduce spending temporarily in down markets (flexible withdrawal rules)
  • Have income from other sources (Social Security, part-time work, rental income)
  • Consider a small SPIA for baseline income

Inflation Protection

Inflation is the silent killer of retirement plans. Tools to fight it:

  • TIPS (Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities): Principal adjusts with CPI. Yields are low but guaranteed to keep pace with inflation. Allocate 10‚Äì20% of bonds to TIPS.
  • I-Bonds: Currently offering competitive rates. $10,000/year purchase limit per person ($20K for a couple). Great for the cash bucket.
  • Stocks: Historically the best long-term inflation hedge. Companies raise prices and earnings with inflation.
  • Social Security: Includes annual COLA adjustments ‚Äî another reason to delay claiming for a higher base benefit.

9. Test Drive It

Live on Your Retirement Budget

Before you pull the trigger, live on your projected retirement budget for 6‚Äì12 months while still working. If you plan to spend $80K/year in retirement ($6,667/month), limit yourself to that now. Bank everything extra. This reveals hidden expenses, tests your discipline, and shows whether the number actually works in practice. Most people discover they either spend more than expected (eating out, subscriptions, home maintenance) or have room to spare.

Tracking Tools

  • YNAB (You Need A Budget): Best for active budgeting and expense tracking ($99/year)
  • Empower (formerly Personal Capital): Best free tool for tracking investments, net worth, and retirement projections
  • FIRECalc / cFIREsim: Free Monte Carlo retirement simulators
  • Social Security.gov: Create an account to see your projected benefits
  • Boldin (formerly NewRetirement): Detailed retirement planning tool ($120/year, worth it)

Psychological Preparation

This is the part most people skip, and it matters enormously:

  • Identity beyond work: If your identity is wrapped up in your career, retirement can trigger a real identity crisis. Start building non-work identity now ‚Äî hobbies, community, volunteering, relationships.
  • Daily structure: Unstructured days sound great for a month. After that, many retirees feel lost. Have a plan: morning routine, projects, exercise, social activities.
  • Social connections: Work provides built-in social interaction. Without it, isolation is a real risk. Join clubs, volunteer, take classes, maintain friendships actively.
  • Purpose: Studies consistently show that retirees with a sense of purpose live longer and are happier. This doesn‚Äôt have to be grand ‚Äî gardening, mentoring, building things, learning new skills all count.

What Most People Get Wrong

  1. Underestimating healthcare costs: The biggest gap between ‚ÄúI‚Äôll be fine‚Äù and reality. Budget more than you think.
  2. Overestimating Social Security: It replaces about 40% of pre-retirement income for average earners. It‚Äôs a supplement, not a plan.
  3. Ignoring inflation: $80K/year at 60 needs to be $120K+ at 75 to maintain the same lifestyle.
  4. Not testing the plan: Spreadsheets aren‚Äôt reality. Live it before you commit.
  5. Retiring FROM something instead of TO something: The happiest retirees have a clear vision of what they want to do, not just what they want to escape.
  6. Forgetting about taxes: A $2M portfolio isn‚Äôt $2M if it‚Äôs all in a traditional IRA ‚Äî the IRS owns 20‚Äì30% of it.
