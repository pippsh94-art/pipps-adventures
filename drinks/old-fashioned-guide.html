The Old Fashioned: America's First Cocktail 🥃

Spirit, sugar, bitters, ice — the drink that started it all

The Origin Story

The Old Fashioned is arguably the oldest cocktail in existence. Its roots trace back to the early 1800s when the word "cocktail" was literally defined as a drink made of spirit, sugar, water, and bitters — exactly what an Old Fashioned is today.

The drink gained its name in the 1880s at the Pendennis Club in Louisville, Kentucky, where old-school drinkers grew frustrated with bartenders experimenting with liqueurs and fruit juices. They started ordering their whiskey "the old-fashioned way" — just spirit, sugar, bitters, and water. The name stuck.

By the early 1900s it was the most popular cocktail in America. It fell out of favor during Prohibition (1920–1933), returned with cheap bourbon after repeal, and has since been fully reclaimed as a bartender benchmark and a whiskey lover's standard. If a bar can't make a good Old Fashioned, walk out.

The Old Fashioned Fundamentals

  • The Spirit: Traditionally Bourbon or Rye whiskey. Bourbon is sweeter and softer. Rye is spicier and drier. Both are correct.
  • The Sweetener: Simple syrup is consistent. Sugar cube is traditional. Demerara adds depth. Maple, honey, or agave open up variations.
  • The Bitters: Angostura is the classic backbone. Orange bitters are a natural add. Don't skip them — bitters are what make this a cocktail, not just whiskey on the rocks.
  • The Dilution: Critical. Stir, don't shake. A large, dense ice cube melts slowly, chilling without over-diluting. The stir is what integrates everything.
  • The Garnish: Orange peel expressed over the drink. The oils released are part of the flavor profile, not decoration.

The Classic Old Fashioned

This is the bedrock. Master this and you can build anything.

Ingredients & Ratios

Ingredient Amount Notes
Bourbon or Rye Whiskey 2 oz Bourbon for sweeter, Rye for spicier
Simple Syrup (1:1) ¼ oz Equal parts sugar and hot water, stir to dissolve
Angostura Bitters 2-3 dashes The backbone — don't skip
Orange Peel 1 strip For expressed oils and garnish
Large Ice Cube 1 Or sphere — slow melt, less dilution

Instructions

  1. Chill Your Glass: Place rocks glass in freezer for 5 minutes or fill with ice water while you prep
  2. Combine: In the glass, add simple syrup and bitters
  3. Add Whiskey: Pour 2 oz of your chosen spirit
  4. Stir: Add a few small ice cubes, stir gently for 30-60 seconds to combine and chill
  5. Ice: Discard stir ice, add your large cube or sphere
  6. Stir Again: Stir slowly for another 20-30 seconds. Taste. Adjust.
  7. Garnish: Hold orange peel skin-side down over the glass and give it a sharp bend to express the oils over the surface, then drop it in or drape over the rim

The Bartender's Method: Level Up Your Old Fashioned 🎥

Tips from a seasoned bartender on building your personal perfect Old Fashioned

1. Choose High Proof + High Rye

Go for at least 100 proof (50% ABV) — the extra strength holds up against dilution and keeps the drink bold and robust. For bourbon, look for high-rye mash bills (20%+ rye content) to add spice and complexity. Old Forester 100 proof is a great starting point.

2. Upgrade Your Sweetener: Rich Demerara Gum Syrup

Skip basic 1:1 simple syrup. Use a rich demerara syrup (2:1 sugar:water) — the higher sugar ratio adds body and texture. Demerara is less refined than white sugar, so it carries natural notes of molasses, caramel, and dark chocolate that pair beautifully with aged spirits.

Even better: look for a syrup made with gum arabic (a natural sap extract). Gum arabic was a standard ingredient in old-school bar syrups and adds a silky, luxurious mouthfeel. Liber & Co. Demerara Syrup checks all these boxes.

  • Amount: ¼ oz (7.5ml) — start here and adjust to taste

3. Split Your Bitters

Instead of 2-3 dashes of Angostura only, split between Angostura and a second bitter to add complexity:

  • Angostura + Orange Bitters — bright citrus lift, pairs great with bourbon
  • Angostura + Chocolate Bitters — deep, rich, and warming
  • Match your second bitters to the whiskey you're using

4. Intentionally Underdilute

Since you're serving over ice, err on the side of less dilution when stirring. An overdiluted drink is hard to fix. An underdiluted one will evolve in the glass as the ice melts — letting the drink open up over time the way a good whiskey should.

5. Match Your Garnish to Your Whiskey

  • Bourbon → Orange peel — the citrus oils complement the sweeter grain profile
  • Rye → Lemon peel — brighter acidity mirrors the drier spice
  • High-rye Bourbon → Orange + a touch of lemon — best of both worlds

Always express the peel over the glass to release the oils — that's flavor, not decoration.

Old Fashioned Variations

1. The Smoked Old Fashioned ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

Adds a beautiful smoky aroma and visual theater. The crowd-pleaser version.

Ingredient Amount Notes
Bourbon or Rye 2 oz Bold, higher-proof bourbon stands up well to smoke (try Knob Creek)
Demerara Syrup (2:1) ¼ oz 2 parts Demerara sugar to 1 part hot water — richer than simple syrup
Angostura Bitters 2 dashes Classic backbone
Orange Bitters 1 dash Complements smoke and orange peel nicely
Orange Peel 1 strip Garnish
Large Ice Cube 1
Smoking Wood Chips Pinch Cherry, pecan, oak, or hickory — cherry is most popular
Instructions:
  1. Build the drink in the glass — syrup, bitters, whiskey, stir briefly
  2. Add large ice cube
  3. Using a handheld smoker, introduce smoke under a cloche or inverted glass placed over the drink
  4. Let smoke infuse for 30-60 seconds, then lift the cloche tableside for the reveal
  5. Stir gently, express orange peel, serve immediately

2. The Mezcal Old Fashioned ⭐⭐⭐⭐

A modern smoky twist. Swap the whiskey for mezcal and you get something agave-forward, earthy, and complex.

Ingredient Amount Notes
Mezcal (Espadín) 2 oz Banhez, Del Maguey Vida, or Ilegal Joven are solid choices
Agave Nectar ¼ oz Complements mezcal's natural earthiness — don't use simple syrup
Mole Bitters 2 dashes Or chocolate bitters — adds depth and chocolate undertones
Orange Bitters 1 dash Brightens and lifts the spice
Orange Peel 1 strip Garnish
Large Ice Cube 1
Instructions:
  1. Add agave nectar, mole bitters, and orange bitters to glass
  2. Add mezcal, stir briefly to combine
  3. Add large ice cube, stir for 30 seconds
  4. Express orange peel, garnish, serve

3. The Maple Old Fashioned ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Perfect for fall and winter. The maple adds warmth and richness that standard simple syrup can't match.

Ingredient Amount Notes
Rye Whiskey 2 oz Rye's spice pairs wonderfully with maple — try Bulleit or Rittenhouse
Maple Syrup (Grade A Dark) ¼ - ½ oz Real maple syrup only — not pancake syrup
Peychaud's Bitters 2 dashes Anise note, slight pink hue — works great with maple
Angostura Bitters 1 dash For balance and depth
Lemon Peel 1 strip Brighter than orange peel — lifts the sweetness
Large Ice Cube 1
Instructions:
  1. Add maple syrup and both bitters to glass
  2. Add rye whiskey, stir briefly
  3. Add large ice cube, stir for 40-60 seconds — maple needs more integration time
  4. Express lemon peel, garnish, serve

4. The Black Manhattan (Amaretto Old Fashioned) ⭐⭐⭐

A richer, darker variation using Averna Amaro in place of simple syrup. Complex, slightly bitter, adult.

Ingredient Amount Notes
Bourbon 2 oz Something full-bodied — Four Roses or Woodford work well
Averna Amaro ½ oz Replaces the sweetener — herbal, slightly bitter, rich
Angostura Bitters 2 dashes Classic backbone
Orange Peel 1 strip Garnish
Large Ice Cube 1
Instructions:
  1. Add Averna and bitters to glass
  2. Add bourbon, stir briefly
  3. Add large ice cube, stir for 30-40 seconds
  4. Express orange peel, garnish, serve

Old Fashioned Philosophy & Technique

Stir, Never Shake

The Old Fashioned is a stirred drink. Shaking introduces air bubbles, dilutes too fast, and makes the drink cloudy. This isn't a preference — it's the correct technique for spirit-forward cocktails. Stir slowly and deliberately, pulling the bar spoon around the inside of the glass in long, fluid circles.

The Ice Matters

Large format ice (2-inch cubes or spheres) is the standard for a reason. Surface area determines melt rate — big ice = slow melt = controlled dilution = your drink stays cold and properly balanced for the whole pour. Small crushed ice is for tiki drinks and juleps, not Old Fashioneds.

The Orange Peel Technique

Cut a wide strip of orange peel — about 1 inch wide and 2-3 inches long — making sure to get the outer skin without too much white pith. Hold the peel skin-side down about 4-6 inches above the glass and give it a sharp bend to snap it. You'll see and smell the oils misting over the drink. That's the point. Then rub the peel around the rim and drop it in.

The Correct Glass

A rocks glass (also called an Old Fashioned glass or lowball). Heavy bottom, wide mouth, typically 6-10 oz. The wide mouth lets the aroma of the orange oils and whiskey breathe up to your nose as you drink. A tall glass or narrow glass ruins the experience.

Common Mistakes

  • Adding soda water: Not a thing. That's a Wisconsin Old Fashioned (different drink). If someone adds soda water, walk away.
  • Muddling fruit: The original recipe never had fruit muddled into it. Muddle a cherry and an orange slice and you've made a different drink.
  • Using bad whiskey: The whiskey is 90% of the drink. Don't use rail spirits.
  • Under-stirring: 30-60 seconds minimum. The drink is not integrated until it's properly stirred.
  • Over-sweetening: ¼ oz syrup is the starting point. More is a dessert drink. Adjust to taste but don't go beyond ½ oz.

Food Pairings

  • Smoked Meats: BBQ brisket, smoked ribs — whiskey and smoke are natural partners
  • Dark Chocolate: 70%+ cacao — the bitters and richness mirror each other
  • Aged Cheeses: Sharp cheddar, aged gouda — fat and salt balance the sweetness
  • Charcuterie: Prosciutto, salami, country ham — salt cuts through the spirit
  • Pecan Pie or Toffee: Caramel and nut notes mirror bourbon's natural flavors
  • Walnuts: Simple, effective — great with rye-based Old Fashioneds

Final Thoughts

The Old Fashioned is the measuring stick for cocktails. It has no place to hide — no juice, no liqueur, no cream to mask flaws. If your whiskey is bad, you'll know. If your technique is off, you'll taste it. But when it comes together — proper spirit, correct dilution, expressed orange oils, ice cold — there is no better cocktail.

It's been ordered the same way for nearly 200 years. There's a reason for that.

Stir it right, drink it cold.
