The Martini: Gin's Greatest Expression 🍸

Cold, clear, three ingredients, zero margin for error

The Origin Story

The Martini's exact birth is one of cocktailing's great unanswered questions. Three credible origin stories compete: the "Martinez" cocktail served in San Francisco around 1862 (gin, sweet vermouth, maraschino, bitters); the Knickerbocker Hotel bartender Martini di Arma di Taggia in 1911; and the small town of Martinez, California, which has a literal historical marker claiming the drink's invention. The truth is probably that the Martini evolved gradually from the sweeter Martinez cocktail through the late 1800s, getting drier and drier as gin quality improved and people developed a taste for it.

By 1900 the recipe was recognizable: gin and dry vermouth, stirred, served up. Early ratios were lush — 2:1 or even 1:1 gin to vermouth. The drink got progressively drier through the 20th century. By the 1950s, 5:1 was standard. By the Mad Men era of the early 1960s, drinkers were ordering "extra dry" with just a vermouth rinse. Hemingway claimed to drink them 15:1. Winston Churchill reportedly preferred to glance at the vermouth bottle from across the room.

Then came James Bond, and everything got slightly weird. Ian Fleming's Casino Royale (1953) gave us the Vesper — gin, vodka, and Lillet Blanc — and the immortal "shaken, not stirred." Bartenders have spent the seventy years since explaining that you should absolutely stir a Martini, not shake it, because shaking introduces air bubbles, clouds the drink, and ruins the silky cold texture that makes a Martini what it is. Bond was wrong. He was also fictional.

Today the Martini is the most contested cocktail in the world. Everyone has an opinion. Gin or vodka? Olive or twist? How much vermouth? Stirred or shaken? The arguments never end. Which is exactly the point — it's a drink with three ingredients and infinite expression.

The Martini Fundamentals

  • The Gin: London Dry is classic — juniper-forward, dry, structured. The gin is the drink. Buy something you'd drink neat.
  • The Vermouth: Dry (white) vermouth, freshness above all else. Refrigerate after opening, use within 2-3 months. Old vermouth is the #1 cause of bad home Martinis.
  • The Temperature: Cold. Then colder. Then colder still. Glass in the freezer. Gin in the freezer. Mixing glass chilled. The drink should hurt your fingers.
  • The Ratio: Modern standard is around 5:1 gin to vermouth. Old-school wet was 2:1 or 3:1. Bone dry is just a rinse. There is no wrong answer — only your preference.
  • The Method: Stirred, never shaken. Stir 30-40 seconds in a chilled mixing glass with ice.
  • The Garnish: Olive (savory, briny) or lemon twist (bright, citrus oil). Each makes a different drink. Some bars do both — ask for "with a twist and an olive" if you can't choose.

The Classic Dry Martini

The 5:1 standard. Memorize it.

Ingredients & Ratios

Ingredient Amount Notes
London Dry Gin 2½ oz Tanqueray, Beefeater, or Plymouth — straight from the freezer
Dry Vermouth ½ oz Dolin Dry or Noilly Prat — fresh, refrigerated
Olive or Lemon Twist 1-3 olives or 1 twist Castelvetrano olives or a wide lemon peel

Instructions

  1. Pre-Chill Everything: Coupe glass in the freezer at least 15 minutes. Gin in the freezer. Mixing glass chilled with ice water if possible.
  2. Combine: In a chilled mixing glass, add the gin and vermouth
  3. Add Ice: Fill the mixing glass with ice — large, dense cubes are ideal
  4. Stir: 30-40 seconds, smooth and steady. The mixing glass should become uncomfortably cold to hold.
  5. Strain: Strain into the chilled coupe through a julep strainer
  6. Garnish: For lemon twist — express the oils over the surface of the drink, rub the rim with the peel, drop it in or rest it on the edge. For olives — spear 1-3 olives on a pick and lay across the glass.
  7. Serve Immediately: A Martini is at its peak the moment it's poured. Drink it cold or you've wasted it.

Martini Variations

1. The Wet Martini ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

The pre-Prohibition style — much more vermouth, much more flavor. If you've only had bone-dry Martinis you've never really tasted what this drink can be. The wet Martini is having a major comeback among serious bartenders.

Ingredient Amount Notes
London Dry Gin 2 oz Tanqueray or Plymouth
Dry Vermouth 1 oz 2:1 ratio — fresh Dolin Dry essential
Orange Bitters 1 dash Optional but pre-Prohibition tradition
Lemon Twist 1 Express oils generously

2. The Dirty Martini ⭐⭐⭐⭐

Olive brine added to the glass. Salty, briny, savory — divisive but beloved. Use vodka or gin. Quality of the brine matters as much as the spirit.

Ingredient Amount Notes
Gin or Vodka 2½ oz Vodka is more common for dirty
Dry Vermouth ½ oz Standard amount — don't skip
Olive Brine ½ oz Quality olives = quality brine. Save the brine from a Castelvetrano jar.
Green Olives 2-3 Castelvetrano or Cerignola — never the cheap pimento ones

3. The Vesper ⭐⭐⭐⭐⭐

James Bond's invention from Casino Royale (1953). Three spirits — gin, vodka, and Lillet Blanc. Strong, complex, infamous. Note: Fleming originally specified "Kina Lillet," which was discontinued. Cocchi Americano is the closest modern substitute and is what serious bartenders use.

Ingredient Amount Notes
London Dry Gin 3 oz High-proof preferred — Tanqueray or No. Ten
Vodka 1 oz Polish or French — Belvedere or Grey Goose
Cocchi Americano ½ oz The modern stand-in for the discontinued Kina Lillet
Lemon Twist 1 large Wide peel — express oils generously

Note: The Vesper is the only Martini that's traditionally shaken — Bond's order specifically. Most modern bartenders stir it anyway. Try it both ways and decide.

4. The Gibson ⭐⭐⭐⭐

A Martini with a pickled cocktail onion instead of an olive or twist. The onion adds a subtle sulfurous savoriness that completely changes the drink. Older than you'd think — the Gibson dates to the early 1900s.

Ingredient Amount Notes
London Dry Gin 2½ oz Plymouth's softness pairs especially well with the onion
Dry Vermouth ½ oz Dolin Dry
Pickled Cocktail Onions 2-3 Quality matters — avoid the cheap white ones

5. The Vodka Martini ⭐⭐⭐

Smoother, less botanical, less interesting — but undeniably popular. A different drink entirely from a gin Martini, even though the recipe is identical. Best made with a quality vodka and treated as its own thing.

Ingredient Amount Notes
Premium Vodka 2½ oz Belvedere, Ketel One, or Grey Goose
Dry Vermouth ¼ oz Less than gin Martini — vodka shows it more
Lemon Twist or Olive 1 Personal preference

Martini Philosophy & Technique

Stir, Don't Shake (Bond Was Wrong)

The Martini is the most famous shaken-not-stirred drink in history, and it's wrong. Shaking a Martini introduces air bubbles, makes the drink cloudy, dilutes it too quickly, and produces tiny ice shards that float on the surface. A properly stirred Martini is silky, clear, viscous, and uniformly cold. A shaken Martini is foamy and watery. James Bond would have lost more fights if he'd stirred. Stir for 30-40 seconds and you'll never shake one again.

Cold Is Everything

A Martini is largely a temperature delivery system for gin. Every degree above ice-cold is a degree of disappointment. Pre-chill: the glass (freezer, 15+ minutes), the gin (freezer is fine — it won't freeze at 80 proof), the mixing glass (rinse with ice water). Use enough ice in the mixing glass that the drink chills properly without over-stirring. The finished drink should make your fingers tingle.

Vermouth Is the Whole Game

Most home Martinis fail because of dead vermouth. Vermouth is fortified wine — it oxidizes the moment you open it. After 2-3 months at room temperature, an open bottle is dead. Refrigerate it the second you open it, label it with the date, and treat it like the perishable wine it is. Buy half-bottles. The single biggest improvement you can make to your Martinis is fresh vermouth.

The Garnish Is the Drink

Olive vs. twist isn't a small choice. A lemon twist adds bright citrus oils that completely transform the aromatic experience — Martini suddenly becomes fresh, sunny, alive. An olive (or three) adds salinity, umami, and weight — Martini becomes savory, dinner-ready, brooding. They are two different drinks. Try the same gin/vermouth ratio with each, back to back, and you'll be shocked at the difference.

Glass Choice Matters

The classic V-shaped martini glass is photogenic but bad — it spills, the drink warms quickly, and the wide surface area kills aromatics. A coupe (curved bowl, low stem) or a Nick & Nora (small tulip) is what serious bars use. Both keep the drink colder longer and concentrate the aroma. Find a vintage coupe at a thrift store and you'll never look back.

Common Mistakes

  • Old vermouth: The single biggest home killer. Fresh vermouth is non-negotiable.
  • Shaking: Cloudy, watery, wrong. Stir.
  • Warm glass: A Martini in a room-temperature glass is half-dead before you take the first sip.
  • Cheap gin: The gin is 90% of the drink. Buy something you'd happily sip neat.
  • Skipping vermouth: "Bone dry" Martinis (no vermouth) are just cold gin. Cold gin is fine. It's not a Martini.
  • Wrong garnish: Cheap olives or those neon onions ruin the entire drink. Quality matters.
  • Big glass: A 10-oz V-shape is too big. Pour 3 oz, drink it cold, then make another.

Food Pairings

  • Oysters: The classic pairing — both briny, both cold, both built for each other
  • Olives and Almonds: Simple, salty, perfect aperitif food
  • Smoked Salmon: The fat and salt balance the dry gin beautifully
  • Caviar: If you're being decadent, this is the move — vodka Martini for purists
  • Aged Hard Cheese: Parmigiano, manchego, aged gouda
  • Cured Meats: Prosciutto, soppressata, jamón ibérico
  • Steak Tartare: The classic continental pairing — cold, savory, sophisticated

Final Thoughts

The Martini is the most argued-about cocktail in history. People fight about gin vs. vodka, ratios, garnishes, glassware, temperature, and whether James Bond should be ignored. The arguments are part of the fun. There's no single right Martini — only the one that's right for you, in this glass, on this evening.

What matters: cold gin, fresh vermouth, a properly chilled glass, a stir long enough to integrate but not over-dilute, and the right garnish for your mood. Get those right and the Martini will reward you for the rest of your drinking life.

It's the drink that defined the cocktail age, the drink James Bond made famous, the drink Hemingway drank in Cuba and Churchill drank in London and every Mad Men executive drank for lunch. Three ingredients. Infinite expression. No place to hide.

Cold as the truth. Clear as a confession.
