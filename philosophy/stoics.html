Stoicism

Daily practices and wisdom from the porch where it all began

What Is Stoicism?

Founded in Athens around 300 BC by Zeno of Citium — who taught from a painted porch (the Stoa Poikilē, where the name comes from) — Stoicism is less a belief system than an operating manual for the mind.

The core idea: you don't control what happens to you, only how you respond to it. Everything else — wealth, reputation, the opinions of others, the weather, the traffic, the past — is outside your control. The Stoic trains himself to care only about what he can actually steer: his own judgment, his own actions, his own character.

It's a system for living. Marcus Aurelius ran the Roman Empire while practicing it daily. Two thousand years later, the practices still work.

The Three Pillars

Marcus Aurelius

121–180 AD · Emperor of Rome

Wrote Meditations as a private journal — never intended for publication. The most powerful man on earth, reminding himself every morning to be patient, humble, and just.

Seneca

4 BC–65 AD · Statesman, playwright

Adviser to Nero, eventually forced to take his own life. His letters and essays are practical, direct, and modern — a businessman's Stoicism.

Epictetus

50–135 AD · Born a slave

Crippled, enslaved, eventually freed — taught that freedom is internal. The Enchiridion ("Handbook") is the shortest, sharpest Stoic text ever written.

🧘 Daily Practices

Not just philosophy — a system for living. Marcus Aurelius ruled the Roman Empire while practicing these disciplines daily.

☠️ Memento Mori — Remember That You Will Die
The Stoic anchor. Meditate on death — not with dread, but with clarity. When you remember life is finite, trivial things fall away. What remains is what actually matters. Every morning: "I could die today. What would I do differently?"
🌅 Morning Visualization — Rehearse Your Day
Before the day begins, mentally walk through it. What challenges will arise? How will you respond? Who will test your patience? Prepare your mind before the world gets a vote. This is not worry — it is preparation.
👁️ Prosochē — Attention to the Present
"Pay attention to the day-to-day activities." Stay present in every moment. The Stoic practice of Prosochē is radical presence — not drifting into the past or future, but fully inhabiting the now. Most suffering happens in the imagination. The present moment rarely needs your panic.
🧹 Disentanglement — Steadfast Purpose
Daily strife will pull at you — gossip, politics, other people's drama, petty conflicts. The Stoic practice is to maintain a steadfast purpose to disentangle from it. You can observe without being consumed. You can care without being controlled.
📓 Evening Journal — The Daily Reckoning
Marcus Aurelius kept his Meditations as private journal entries — not for publication, but for self-examination. Each evening, ask three questions:
  • What did I do wrong? — Name it. Condemn the action, not the self. Learn.
  • What did I do correctly? — Acknowledge it without pride. Build on it.
  • What did I miss today? — Where was I absent, inattentive, or small?
📈 Kaizen — Daily Incremental Improvement
From Japanese philosophy, adopted by Stoic practice: 1% better every day. Not dramatic transformation — tiny, consistent improvement in virtue, skill, and character. Compounded over years, it becomes unrecognizable growth. The Stoics called this prokoptō — moral progress.
🌌 View from Above — The Cosmic Perspective
A meditation: rise above your life. See yourself from the sky — a small figure in a vast city, in a vast country, on a pale blue dot. Your problem — how big is it now? Your grudge — does it survive the altitude? Marcus used this to dissolve ego and regain proportion. The universe is indifferent. That is freedom, not tragedy.
