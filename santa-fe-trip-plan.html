Santa Fe, New Mexico — Trip Plan ✈️

A detailed day-by-day trip plan for an 18ft RV adventure from Star, Idaho to Santa Fe and northern New Mexico. Summer/Fall 2026.

Trip Overview

DetailInfo
Starting PointStar, Idaho
DestinationSanta Fe, NM & surrounding areas
Distance~1,000 miles one way
Total Trip10–14 days recommended
Best TimeLate September – early October (golden aspens, Balloon Fiesta, monsoons ending, perfect weather)
Rig18ft self-contained RV / camper

Day 1: Star, ID → Moab, UT (6.5 hours driving)

Route

Star → I-84 south → Twin Falls → I-15 south → I-70 east → Moab

Along the Way

  • Shoshone Falls (Twin Falls) — quick 30-min detour for the "Niagara of the West"
  • Fuel up in Green River, UT before heading south to Moab

Evening

  • Camp: Free BLM dispersed camping along Hwy 128 (Colorado River corridor) or Willow Springs Road area
  • Sunset over red rock canyon walls from your campsite

Don't Miss

  • Drive the Colorado River Scenic Byway (Hwy 128) on the way in — stunning red rock canyon along the river

Day 2: Moab Day (Optional — or drive on)

If you have time, spend a day in Moab. If not, head straight to Santa Fe (7 hours).

If You Stay

  • Morning: Arches National Park — Delicate Arch viewpoint, Windows Section, Double Arch
  • Afternoon: Dead Horse Point State Park — one of the most photographed viewpoints in Utah
  • Evening: Dinner in Moab, beers at Moab Brewery

If You Drive On

  • Moab → I-70 east → US-191 south → I-40 east → I-25 north → Santa Fe
  • Long drive day but beautiful — Colorado Plateau to the Sangre de Cristos

Day 3: Arrive Santa Fe — Explore the City

Camp Setup

  • Hyde Memorial State Park — 20 minutes from the plaza, 8,300 ft in the pines, $10–$14/night
  • Or Black Canyon Campground (Santa Fe NF) — 30 min, free–$10/night, more secluded

Afternoon & Evening — Santa Fe Essentials

  • Santa Fe Plaza — Walk the historic center, browse Native American jewelry under the Palace of the Governors portal
  • Canyon Road — Stroll half a mile of 100+ art galleries in adobe buildings (free to browse)
  • Dinner: The Shed (famous red chile enchiladas) or Tomasita's (green chile everything). Get there early — lines form.
  • Evening: Walk the plaza at dusk, grab a margarita at La Fonda rooftop bar

Day 4: Santa Fe Culture Day

Morning

  • Meow Wolf (House of Eternal Return) — Immersive art experience like nothing else. Walk through a portal in a Victorian house into surreal worlds. Tickets (2026): $52 adult, $41 children 10–13, $22 New Mexico resident. Book ahead at tickets.meowwolf.com — frequently sells out. Plan 2–3 hours.

Afternoon

  • Museum Hill — Museum of International Folk Art (incredible miniature collections) and Museum of Indian Arts & Culture
  • Or Georgia O'Keeffe Museum downtown — small but powerful collection

Evening

  • Ten Thousand Waves — Japanese-style outdoor hot spring spa in the mountains above town. Reservations recommended. Soak under the stars in a cedar-lined tub. Worth the splurge.
  • Or catch a performance at the Santa Fe Opera (if visiting June–August) — open-air opera house with mountain sunset views. Tailgate in the parking lot before the show (a beloved local tradition).

Day 5: Bandelier & Tent Rocks

Morning — Bandelier National Monument (50 min from Santa Fe)

  • Main Loop Trail (1.4 miles) — Walk among ancient cliff dwellings, climb wooden ladders into cave rooms carved by the ancestral Puebloans 700+ years ago
  • Alcove House — Climb 140 ft of ladders to a reconstructed kiva in a natural alcove. Not for the faint of heart, but incredible.
  • Allow 2–3 hours

Afternoon — Kasha-Katuwe Tent Rocks (45 min from Santa Fe)

  • Cave Loop + Canyon Trail (3 miles) — Walk through a narrow slot canyon among volcanic tent-shaped formations, then climb to a panoramic overlook. One of the most unique hikes in the Southwest.
  • ⚠️ Check if open — Cochiti Pueblo controls access and occasionally closes it.

Evening

  • Return to Hyde Memorial SP or head toward the Jemez Mountains for tomorrow
  • Pick up green chile stew at a Santa Fe roadside stand on the way back

Day 6: Jemez Mountains — Hot Springs & Volcanoes

Route

Santa Fe → Hwy 4 west → Jemez Springs → Valles Caldera → loop back (1.5 hrs each way)

Morning

  • Valles Caldera National Preserve — 13-mile wide volcanic caldera. Enormous grassy meadow surrounded by mountains, often with elk herds grazing. Stop at the visitor center and take a short hike. Surreal landscape.

Afternoon

  • Jemez Falls — Short walk to a beautiful waterfall in the pines
  • Soda Dam — Pull over and see this bizarre natural dam of mineral deposits across the Jemez River. Free, roadside.
  • Soak — Options:
    • Spence Hot Springs (free, natural hillside pools — 5-min uphill hike, clothing optional common)
    • Jemez Hot Springs / Giggling Springs ($18–$25, developed pools, more relaxing)
    • San Antonio Hot Springs (free, remote, tiered forest pools — rough road + 1-mi hike, worth it if you're adventurous)

Evening

  • Camp: Jemez Falls Campground ($12/night, forested, quiet) or San Antonio Campground ($10/night)
  • Campfire under the pines in the caldera

Day 7: High Road to Taos

Route

Santa Fe → Chimayo → Truchas → Taos (80 miles, half-day scenic drive with stops)

This is one of the most beautiful drives in New Mexico — winding mountain roads through traditional Spanish villages that haven't changed much in centuries.

Stops Along the Way

  • Chimayo — Visit the Santuario de Chimayo, the "Lourdes of America." Tiny adobe pilgrimage church with "holy dirt" believed to have healing powers. Buy a chile ristra at a roadside stand.
  • Truchas — Tiny mountain village with sweeping views of the Sangre de Cristo peaks. This is where the movie "Milagro Beanfield War" was filmed.
  • Trampas — 18th-century mission church (San José de Gracia). One of the finest Spanish colonial churches in the US.

Afternoon — Taos

  • Taos Pueblo — UNESCO World Heritage Site, continuously inhabited for 1,000+ years. Multi-story adobe buildings. Entry $16. Respect photography rules — ask before shooting, some areas are off-limits.
  • Taos Plaza — Browse galleries and shops. Smaller and more intimate than Santa Fe.

Evening

  • Camp: Orilla Verde Recreation Area (BLM, on the Rio Grande, $7–$15/night) — dramatic volcanic gorge setting
  • Or Cuchilla Campground (Carson NF, free–$7, forested)

Day 8: Rio Grande Gorge & Taos Area

Morning

  • Rio Grande Gorge Bridge — Walk across the 650-ft-high bridge spanning the 800-ft deep gorge. Free. Vertigo-inducing views straight down to the Rio Grande. One of the most dramatic viewpoints in NM.
  • West Rim Trail — Hike along the gorge rim for more views (as far as you want, up to 9 miles one way)

Afternoon — Choose Your Adventure

Option A: Wheeler Peak (highest point in NM, 13,167 ft)

  • 8 miles RT, strenuous. Start early (lightning risk in afternoon). Above treeline with alpine tundra. Stunning 360° views.
  • Trailhead at Taos Ski Valley (Williams Lake TH)

Option B: Rafting the Rio Grande

  • Half-day raft trip through the Taos Box — Class III–IV whitewater in a 800-ft deep gorge. Multiple outfitters in Taos ($50–$100/person). An unforgettable experience.

Option C: Relax

  • Ojo Caliente Mineral Springs — 45 min south of Taos. Historic hot springs with multiple mineral pools (iron, arsenic, lithia, soda). Adobe architecture. Day soak $24–$54. Pure relaxation.

Evening

  • Dinner in Taos — Orlando's (New Mexican classics) or Love Apple (farm-to-table in a chapel)
  • Return to camp

Day 9: Enchanted Circle Drive

Route

84-mile loop: Taos → Angel Fire → Eagle Nest → Red River → Questa → Taos

Highlights

  • Angel Fire — Vietnam Veterans Memorial (moving hilltop memorial)
  • Eagle Nest Lake — Mountain lake with fishing. Stop for a break.
  • Red River — Old West mining town turned ski resort. Charming main street. Grab lunch.
  • Questa — Gateway to Wild Rivers Recreation Area
  • Wild Rivers Recreation Area (optional detour) — Where the Red River meets the Rio Grande in a deep gorge. Rim camping with incredible views. Short trails descend to the rivers and hot springs.

Afternoon/Evening

  • Complete the loop back to Taos or camp at Wild Rivers ($7/night, first-come)
  • Last Taos dinner — hit whatever you missed

Day 10: South to Chaco Canyon (3 hours from Taos)

Route

Taos → I-25 south → US-550 west → Chaco Canyon access road

⚠️ Important

  • Chaco access road is 13–21 miles of rough dirt/gravel. Passable in 18ft but go slow (25 mph). Do NOT attempt after rain — clay becomes impassable.
  • Check road conditions before going: call the visitor center (505-786-7014)
  • Fill up on gas and water before entering — nothing out here

Afternoon

  • Arrive and set up camp at Gallo Campground ($15/night, reserve Loop A on recreation.gov)
  • Pueblo Bonito — Walk through the largest great house. 600+ rooms, 40 kivas, perfectly aligned with solar and lunar events. Mind-blowing that this was built 1,000 years ago.
  • Casa Rinconada — Great kiva aligned with the summer solstice

Evening — Stargazing ⭐⭐⭐

  • Chaco is an International Dark Sky Park. This is some of the best stargazing in the entire US.
  • The Milky Way arcs overhead in vivid detail. You'll see more stars than you thought existed.
  • Bring a camera with manual settings for astrophotography
  • Ranger-led night sky programs available (check schedule)

Day 11: Chaco Morning → Head Back Toward Santa Fe

Morning

  • Sunrise at Chaco — soft light on ancient stone walls
  • Pueblo del Arroyo and Chetro Ketl — more great houses to explore
  • Petroglyph trails along the canyon walls

Afternoon

  • Drive back to the Santa Fe area (3 hours)
  • Camp option: Return to Hyde Memorial SP or Caja del Rio BLM (free, mesa-top dispersed camping 20 min from Santa Fe)

Evening

  • Final Santa Fe evening — revisit anything you missed
  • Stock up on souvenirs: turquoise jewelry, pottery, chile ristras, Chimayo weavings

Day 12: White Sands Day Trip (Optional — adds 1 day)

If you have extra time, White Sands is 3.5 hours south and absolutely worth it.

Drive

Santa Fe → I-25 south → US-380/US-70 → White Sands National Park

At the Park

  • Dunes Drive — 8-mile scenic road into the heart of the white gypsum dunes
  • Alkali Flat Trail (5 miles) — Walk deep into the dune field. Surreal and otherworldly. Bring lots of water.
  • Sunset — The dunes turn pink, orange, and purple. One of the most beautiful sunsets you'll ever see.
  • Sled the dunes! (Buy a saucer at the gift shop — $20)

Camp

  • Oliver Lee Memorial State Park (30 min from White Sands, $10–$14/night) — dramatic desert canyon at the base of the Sacramento Mountains

Return

  • Drive back to Santa Fe the next day, or head directly home via I-25 north

Days 13–14: Return to Star, ID

Route Home (reverse Day 1–2)

Santa Fe → I-25 north → I-70 west → Moab area → I-15 north → I-84 → Star

Split into 2 days

  • Day 13: Santa Fe → Moab/Green River area (7 hours). Camp one more night in the red rocks.
  • Day 14: Moab → Star (6.5 hours). Home.

Alternate: Southern Route via Flagstaff/Vegas

Santa Fe → I-40 west → Flagstaff → I-15 north → I-84 → Star (longer but passes through Petrified Forest, Flagstaff, and Sedona options)

Pre-Trip Checklist

  • Reserve Gallo Campground Loop A at Chaco (recreation.gov)
  • Reserve Hyde Memorial SP sites (newmexicostateparks.reserveamerica.com)
  • Book Meow Wolf tickets ($52 adult — sells out, especially weekends)
  • Book Ten Thousand Waves spa (optional)
  • Check Santa Fe Opera schedule (June–August only)
  • Buy/renew America the Beautiful Pass ($80/year)
  • Download offline maps (Google Maps / Gaia GPS) — cell service is spotty in rural NM
  • Check Chaco road conditions closer to trip date (505-786-7014)
  • Check Tent Rocks (Kasha-Katuwe) open status with Cochiti Pueblo
  • Fill propane, top off water, check tires before leaving Star

Quick Tips

  • "Red or green?" — Every restaurant will ask. This means red or green chile. "Christmas" = both. Always say Christmas your first time.
  • Altitude: Santa Fe is 7,000 ft. Drink way more water than you think you need. Take it easy Day 1.
  • Monsoons (July–Sept): Storms roll in between 2–5 PM like clockwork. Plan outdoor activities for morning. Never camp in a dry wash.
  • Respect pueblos: They are sovereign nations and active communities, not tourist attractions. Follow all posted rules.
  • Cash: Carry small bills for pueblo entry fees, roadside chile stands, and art purchases
  • Fall timing: If you can go late September / early October — golden aspens, perfect weather, and the Albuquerque Balloon Fiesta (first two weeks of October, 500+ balloons). Peak magic.
