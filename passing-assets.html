1. The Big Picture

Three Ways Assets Transfer at Death

Priority order: Beneficiary designations and trust transfers override your will. If your 401(k) beneficiary form names your ex-spouse, they get it ‚Äî even if your will says otherwise. This is the #1 mistake people make.

Key Tax Concepts

Stepped-Up Basis: When you die, most assets get their cost basis ‚Äústepped up‚Äù to the fair market value at the date of death. If you bought stock at $10 and it‚Äôs worth $100 when you die, your children inherit it at $100 ‚Äî they owe zero capital gains on the $90 of growth. This is one of the most powerful tax benefits in the entire tax code.

Estate Tax: Federal estate tax only applies to estates over $13.61 million per person / $27.22 million per couple (2024). Unless you‚Äôre above this, federal estate tax is not a concern. Idaho has no state estate tax.

Income Tax on Inherited Accounts: Different assets have very different tax treatment when inherited. This is where strategy matters most.

2. Homes & Real Estate

Best Options (Ranked)

Option 1: Revocable Living Trust ‚≠ê (Best for Most People)

How: Transfer the deed to your trust now. At death, successor trustee distributes per trust terms.

Pros: - Avoids probate entirely - Private ‚Äî no public record - Immediate transfer ‚Äî kids don‚Äôt wait months - Home gets full stepped-up basis at death (huge capital gains savings) - In Idaho (community property state), both halves of a jointly-owned home get stepped up at the first spouse‚Äôs death

Cons: - Costs $1,500‚Äì$3,000 to set up (one-time, with full estate plan) - Must remember to actually transfer the deed

Example: You bought your home for $200,000. It‚Äôs worth $600,000 when you pass. Your children inherit it with a $600,000 basis. If they sell for $620,000, they only pay capital gains on $20,000 ‚Äî not $420,000.

Option 2: Transfer on Death Deed (TOD Deed)

How: Record a TOD deed that automatically transfers the home to named beneficiaries at death. You keep full ownership and control while alive.

Pros: - Avoids probate - Simple and cheap ($50‚Äì$200 to record) - Revocable ‚Äî you can change it anytime - Home still gets stepped-up basis

Cons: - Idaho adopted TOD deeds in 2019 ‚Äî so this is available to you - Doesn‚Äôt provide incapacity protection (unlike a trust) - Can get complicated with multiple beneficiaries or if a beneficiary dies before you

Best for: People who want a simple probate-avoidance tool without the cost of a full trust.

Option 3: Joint Tenancy with Right of Survivorship

How: Add children to the deed as joint tenants.

‚ö†Ô∏è Generally NOT recommended: - Triggers a gift (potential gift tax issues on amounts over $18,000/year per person) - Children don‚Äôt get full stepped-up basis ‚Äî they inherit your original cost basis for your share - Exposes the home to your children‚Äôs creditors, lawsuits, and divorces - If your child has financial problems, a lien can be placed on YOUR home - Could affect your child‚Äôs financial aid eligibility - Capital gains tax hit when they sell

‚ùå Option 4: Quitclaim Deed (Gift While Alive)

Do NOT do this. Gifting your home before death means: - Children get your original cost basis (no step-up) ‚Äî potentially massive capital gains tax - You lose the $250K/$500K primary residence capital gains exclusion - You lose control of your home - Potential gift tax implications - Possible Medicaid lookback issues

Bottom line: Let your home transfer at death for the stepped-up basis. Don‚Äôt gift it early.

3. Cash & Bank Accounts

Best Options (Ranked)

Option 1: Payable on Death (POD) Designation ‚≠ê

How: Go to your bank and add a POD beneficiary to each account. At death, the beneficiary brings a death certificate and the account transfers immediately.

Pros: - Avoids probate - Free to set up - Instant transfer - You keep full control while alive ‚Äî beneficiary has zero access until your death - No tax consequences (cash doesn‚Äôt appreciate, so no capital gains issue)

Cons: - Equal splits can be awkward (need to split across accounts or name percentages) - Doesn‚Äôt provide incapacity protection

Option 2: Hold in a Revocable Trust

How: Retitle bank accounts in the name of your trust. Distributed per trust terms at death.

Pros: - Avoids probate - Incapacity protection (successor trustee manages if you can‚Äôt) - Can include conditions (age restrictions, staggered distributions, etc.)

Cons: - Slightly more paperwork to set up

Option 3: Joint Account

How: Add child to the account as joint owner.

‚ö†Ô∏è Risky: - Child has immediate access ‚Äî can withdraw everything - Exposed to child‚Äôs creditors and lawsuits - Could be considered a gift for tax purposes - Can create family conflict if one child is on the account but others aren‚Äôt - Only use this if you genuinely want the child to have access NOW (e.g., helping manage your finances)

Tax Impact

  • Cash inheritance is not taxable income to your children
  • Any interest earned after transfer is taxable to them
  • No step-up basis issue (cash is cash)

4. Traditional IRA & 401(k)

‚ö†Ô∏è These Are the Most Tax-Complicated Assets to Inherit

Traditional IRAs and 401(k)s are pre-tax money ‚Äî your children will owe income tax on every dollar they withdraw. This is the biggest tax bill in most inheritances.

The SECURE Act Rules (2020+)

The SECURE Act eliminated the ‚Äústretch IRA‚Äù for most non-spouse beneficiaries. Here‚Äôs how it works now:

Spouse Inherits ‚Üí Best Treatment

  • Can roll into their own IRA ‚Äî treated as if it was always theirs
  • Can delay RMDs until their own required age (73/75)
  • Can name new beneficiaries
  • This is the best outcome for tax deferral

Children Inherit ‚Üí 10-Year Rule

  • Non-spouse beneficiaries must withdraw the entire balance within 10 years of your death
  • If you were already taking RMDs, they must also take annual distributions during the 10 years
  • If you hadn‚Äôt started RMDs, they can wait and take it all in year 10 (but that creates a massive tax bomb)
  • No more lifetime stretch ‚Äî this was a huge change

Exceptions to the 10-Year Rule

These beneficiaries can still stretch over their lifetime: - Surviving spouse - Minor children (until age 21, then 10-year clock starts) - Disabled or chronically ill individuals - Beneficiaries less than 10 years younger than you

Strategies to Minimize the Tax Hit

Strategy 1: Roth Conversions Before Death ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê

The single best thing you can do. Convert traditional IRA/401(k) money to Roth while you‚Äôre alive, especially in early retirement when your tax bracket is low.

  • You pay the tax now at (presumably) a lower rate
  • Your children inherit a Roth IRA ‚Äî still subject to the 10-year rule, but all withdrawals are tax-free
  • A $500,000 Roth IRA inherited is worth $500,000. A $500,000 traditional IRA inherited might only be worth $350,000 after taxes.

Example: - Traditional IRA: $500,000. Children withdraw over 10 years. At a 24% bracket, they net ~$380,000. - Roth IRA: $500,000. You paid tax during conversion at 12-22% (in early retirement). Children get the full $500,000 tax-free. - Savings: $50,000‚Äì$120,000+ in taxes

Strategy 2: Coordinate Withdrawals with Children‚Äôs Income

If your children have variable income (self-employed, between jobs, lower-earning years), they should take larger distributions in low-income years to minimize the tax bracket impact.

Strategy 3: Charitable Beneficiary for IRA

If you‚Äôre charitably inclined, name a charity as the IRA beneficiary (or partial beneficiary). The charity pays zero tax on the IRA distribution. Leave other assets (home, brokerage ‚Äî which get stepped-up basis) to your children instead. This is the most tax-efficient allocation:

  • IRAs ‚Üí Charity (avoids all income tax)
  • Brokerage + Home ‚Üí Children (stepped-up basis, minimal/no capital gains)

Strategy 4: Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD)

After age 70¬Ω, donate up to $105,000/year directly from your IRA to charity. Reduces your IRA balance (less for kids to pay tax on) while satisfying your charitable goals and RMD obligations.

401(k) Specific Notes

  • If still employed past 73, you can delay RMDs on your current employer‚Äôs 401(k)
  • Roll old 401(k)s into an IRA for more flexibility and easier beneficiary management
  • Some 401(k) plans have restrictive beneficiary payout options ‚Äî an IRA gives your kids more control

Naming Beneficiaries ‚Äî Do It Right

  • Primary beneficiary: Spouse (usually)
  • Contingent beneficiary: Children (list each by name and percentage ‚Äî not ‚Äúmy children‚Äù)
  • Review after: Divorce, remarriage, death of a beneficiary, birth of a grandchild
  • Never name your estate as IRA beneficiary ‚Äî forces probate AND may accelerate taxation
  • Never name your trust as IRA beneficiary unless it‚Äôs specifically designed as a ‚Äúconduit‚Äù or ‚Äúaccumulation‚Äù trust (requires attorney setup) ‚Äî otherwise you can lose stretch/deferral benefits

5. Roth IRA & Roth 401(k)

The Best Asset to Leave Your Children ‚≠ê‚≠ê‚≠ê

Why Roth is king for inheritance: - Children still must withdraw within 10 years (SECURE Act) - But every dollar comes out completely tax-free - No income tax, no capital gains tax, nothing - The Roth has already been taxed (you paid tax on contributions/conversions)

Strategy: Prioritize Spending Traditional, Preserving Roth

In retirement, spend down your traditional IRA/401(k) first (or convert to Roth). Leave the Roth to grow tax-free as long as possible. The Roth is the most valuable asset to pass on because of its tax-free status.

Spending order for retirement (optimized for inheritance): 1. Taxable brokerage accounts (get stepped-up basis at death anyway) 2. Traditional IRA/401(k) (reduces future tax burden for kids) 3. Roth IRA (preserve this ‚Äî most valuable inheritance)

Wait ‚Äî that conflicts slightly. The optimal order depends on your tax bracket and goals. A financial advisor can model this for your specific situation. The general principle: Roth is the most tax-efficient asset to leave behind.

Roth 401(k)

  • Roll into a Roth IRA before death if possible ‚Äî gives beneficiaries more flexibility
  • Roth 401(k)s are now exempt from RMDs during your lifetime (SECURE 2.0, effective 2024)
  • Same 10-year rule applies to inherited Roth 401(k)

6. Brokerage / Taxable Investment Accounts

The Second-Best Asset to Leave Your Children

Why: The stepped-up cost basis makes taxable brokerage accounts extremely tax-efficient to inherit.

How It Works

You buy $200,000 of stock. It grows to $800,000. If you sell while alive, you owe capital gains on $600,000 of gains (~$90,000‚Äì$140,000 in taxes).

If you hold until death, your children inherit at the $800,000 stepped-up basis. If they sell the next day for $800,000, they owe $0 in capital gains.

The $600,000 of gains is never taxed. Ever. This is the most powerful tax benefit in estate planning.

Best Transfer Options

Option 1: Transfer on Death (TOD) Registration ‚≠ê

How: Add a TOD beneficiary to your brokerage account (Schwab, Fidelity, Vanguard all offer this). At death, assets transfer directly.

Pros: - Avoids probate - Free to set up - Children get stepped-up basis - You maintain full control while alive

Option 2: Hold in a Revocable Trust

How: Retitle the account in the trust‚Äôs name. Same tax treatment ‚Äî still gets stepped-up basis.

Pros: - Avoids probate - Incapacity protection - Can add conditions (age restrictions, staggered distributions)

Strategy: Hold Appreciated Stock Until Death

Never sell highly appreciated stock in a taxable account if you‚Äôre planning to leave it to your children. The step-up erases all gains.

If you need to sell investments in retirement, sell from these accounts first: 1. Stocks with losses (harvest the loss) 2. Stocks with minimal gains 3. Traditional IRA/401(k) (no step-up benefit anyway)

Hold your biggest winners in the taxable brokerage account ‚Äî let the step-up work its magic.

Idaho Community Property Bonus

Idaho is a community property state. This means both halves of jointly-owned brokerage accounts get a stepped-up basis at the first spouse‚Äôs death ‚Äî not just the deceased spouse‚Äôs half. This is a massive advantage over common-law states.

Example (Community Property State like Idaho): - Couple buys $200,000 of stock. Worth $800,000 at first spouse‚Äôs death. - Surviving spouse‚Äôs basis: $800,000 (full step-up on both halves) - If they sell for $800,000: $0 capital gains

Same example in a Common-Law State: - Only the deceased‚Äôs half gets stepped up - Surviving spouse‚Äôs basis: $500,000 ($100K original half + $400K stepped-up half) - If they sell for $800,000: $300,000 in capital gains ‚Äî potentially $45K‚Äì$70K in taxes

Idaho‚Äôs community property rules save your family potentially tens of thousands of dollars.

7. Life Insurance

Passes Tax-Free (Usually)

Life insurance death benefits are income tax-free to your beneficiaries. A $500,000 policy pays $500,000 ‚Äî no income tax.

However, the death benefit is included in your taxable estate for estate tax purposes. For estates under $13.61M, this doesn‚Äôt matter. For larger estates, an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) removes the policy from your estate.

Best Practices

  • Name beneficiaries directly (avoids probate)
  • Never name your estate as beneficiary ‚Äî forces probate and may create tax issues
  • Name primary AND contingent beneficiaries
  • Review after major life events

Do You Still Need Life Insurance in Retirement?

Maybe not. If your assets are sufficient to support your spouse and pass to your children, life insurance may be unnecessary. Common reasons to keep it: - Mortgage still outstanding ‚Äî insurance covers it - Estate liquidity ‚Äî if most of your wealth is in a home or illiquid assets, insurance provides cash for taxes/expenses - Equalization ‚Äî if one child gets the house and another gets cash, insurance can balance it - Charitable giving ‚Äî leave insurance to charity, other assets to children - Final expenses ‚Äî small policy ($10K‚Äì$25K) for burial, funeral, and estate settlement costs

8. Gifting While Alive

Annual Gift Tax Exclusion (2024)

You can give $18,000 per person per year (2024, indexed for inflation) without any gift tax implications.

  • You + spouse can give $36,000/year to each child
  • If your child is married, you can give $36,000 to them AND $36,000 to their spouse = $72,000/year per couple to each child‚Äôs family
  • No tax return required for gifts under the annual exclusion

Lifetime Gift Tax Exemption

Above the annual exclusion, you can give up to $13.61 million (2024) over your lifetime before any gift tax applies. You must file a gift tax return (Form 709) but won‚Äôt actually owe tax unless you exceed the lifetime exemption.

‚ö†Ô∏è This exemption is shared with your estate tax exemption. Every dollar you gift above the annual exclusion reduces your estate tax exemption dollar-for-dollar.

When Gifting Makes Sense

‚úÖ Gift cash ‚Äî no basis issues, no step-up lost ‚úÖ Gift to 529 plans ‚Äî up to 5 years of annual exclusion at once ($90,000) for grandchildren‚Äôs education ‚úÖ Pay medical bills or tuition directly ‚Äî unlimited, doesn‚Äôt count toward gift limits (must pay the institution directly) ‚úÖ Gift assets expected to appreciate significantly ‚Äî removes future growth from your estate

When Gifting Does NOT Make Sense

‚ùå Don‚Äôt gift appreciated stock or real estate ‚Äî your children get YOUR cost basis (carryover basis), not a stepped-up basis. They‚Äôll owe capital gains when they sell. Let these assets pass at death instead. ‚ùå Don‚Äôt gift your home ‚Äî lose the step-up, lose control, possible Medicaid issues ‚ùå Don‚Äôt gift IRA/401(k) money ‚Äî you can‚Äôt transfer these. You‚Äôd have to withdraw (pay income tax), then gift the cash.

9. Idaho-Specific Considerations

Community Property Advantage

  • Idaho is 1 of 9 community property states
  • Both halves of community property get stepped-up basis at first death
  • This applies to home, brokerage accounts, and other jointly held assets
  • Massive capital gains savings compared to common-law states

No State Estate Tax

  • Idaho does not impose a state estate or inheritance tax
  • Only federal estate tax applies (exemption: $13.61M/person)
  • Some states tax estates as low as $1M ‚Äî Idaho doesn‚Äôt

No State Inheritance Tax

  • Some states tax the recipient of an inheritance ‚Äî Idaho does not
  • Your children receive their inheritance with no Idaho tax consequences

Idaho Income Tax on Inherited IRAs

  • Idaho taxes IRA/401(k) distributions as income (flat 5.695% rate)
  • Children living in Idaho will pay both federal AND Idaho income tax on inherited traditional IRA distributions
  • Children living in no-income-tax states (Nevada, Texas, Florida, etc.) save 5.695% on every dollar
  • Strategy: If a child lives in a no-income-tax state, consider leaving them a larger share of the traditional IRA

Transfer on Death Deeds

  • Idaho adopted TOD deeds in 2019
  • Simple, cheap way to pass real estate without probate
  • File with the county recorder‚Äôs office

10. Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. ‚ùå Not Updating Beneficiary Designations

The #1 estate planning failure. Your ex-spouse is still on your 401(k). Your deceased parent is still your IRA beneficiary. Check every account annually.

2. ‚ùå Naming Your Estate as Beneficiary

Forces probate. For IRAs, may accelerate the distribution timeline and increase taxes. Always name individuals or a properly structured trust.

3. ‚ùå Gifting Appreciated Assets Before Death

You lose the stepped-up basis. Your children pay capital gains you could have erased. Hold appreciated assets until death.

4. ‚ùå Leaving Everything in Traditional IRAs

Your children will owe income tax on every dollar. Convert to Roth during your low-income retirement years. Pay 12‚Äì22% now instead of your kids paying 24‚Äì35% later.

5. ‚ùå Not Having a Trust for the Home

Without a trust or TOD deed, your home goes through probate ‚Äî public, slow, expensive. Easy to avoid.

6. ‚ùå Treating All Children Equally When Tax Situations Differ

If one child earns $200K and another earns $50K, giving them equal shares of a traditional IRA means the higher earner pays much more in taxes. Consider giving the higher earner more brokerage (stepped-up basis) and the lower earner more IRA (taxed at their lower rate).

7. ‚ùå Ignoring the 10-Year Rule on Inherited IRAs

Your children may not realize they have only 10 years to withdraw. A large balance withdrawn in year 10 could push them into the 32‚Äì37% tax bracket. Plan a withdrawal strategy.

8. ‚ùå Not Considering Roth Conversions

Every dollar you convert from traditional to Roth is a dollar your children receive tax-free. Early retirement (ages 60‚Äì67) is the golden window for conversions ‚Äî your income is low, your tax rate is low.

9. ‚ùå Forgetting About Digital Assets

Crypto, online accounts, domain names, digital businesses. Document access and include in your estate plan.

10. ‚ùå DIY Without Professional Review

Online tools are fine for simple situations, but the tax optimization of passing assets ‚Äî especially IRAs ‚Äî to children can save tens of thousands. A one-time consultation ($500‚Äì$1,500) with an estate planning attorney or tax-focused financial planner pays for itself many times over.
