1. Putting Your Home in a Trust

What Is a Living Trust?

A revocable living trust is a legal entity you create while alive. You transfer ownership of your assets (home, bank accounts, investments) into the trust. You remain the trustee (controller) and beneficiary while alive ‚Äî nothing changes day-to-day. But when you pass away, the trust assets transfer to your named beneficiaries without going through probate.

Why Put Your Home in a Trust?

Avoid Probate - Probate is the court process of distributing assets after death. It‚Äôs public, slow (6‚Äì18 months in many states), and expensive (3‚Äì7% of estate value in legal/court fees). - In Idaho, probate is relatively straightforward compared to California or New York, but it still takes time, costs money, and becomes public record. - A home in a trust passes directly to beneficiaries ‚Äî no court, no delay, no public record.

Privacy - Probate is public record ‚Äî anyone can see what you owned and who got it. A trust is private. Nobody outside the trust knows what‚Äôs in it or who inherits.

Incapacity Planning - If you become incapacitated (stroke, dementia, accident), a trust with a named successor trustee allows that person to manage the property immediately ‚Äî no court proceeding needed. Without a trust, your family may need a conservatorship (expensive, slow, court-supervised).

Continuity for Your Family - Your home passes to beneficiaries immediately upon death. No frozen assets, no waiting for court approval. They can live in it, sell it, or rent it right away.

Potential Creditor Protection (Irrevocable Trust) - A revocable trust does NOT protect from creditors while you‚Äôre alive (because you still control it). - An irrevocable trust CAN protect assets from creditors, lawsuits, and even Medicaid spend-down ‚Äî but you give up control. This is a more advanced strategy, usually done with an estate attorney.

What It Doesn‚Äôt Do

  • No tax savings with a revocable living trust ‚Äî it‚Äôs tax-neutral while you‚Äôre alive. Your home still gets a stepped-up cost basis at death (capital gains benefit for heirs).
  • Doesn‚Äôt replace a will ‚Äî you still need a ‚Äúpour-over will‚Äù to catch any assets not in the trust.
  • Doesn‚Äôt protect from your own creditors (revocable trust).

Types of Trusts

Type Control Creditor Protection Tax Benefits Best For
Revocable Living Trust Full control, can change anytime None (while alive) None (while alive) Avoiding probate, incapacity planning, privacy
Irrevocable Trust Give up control Yes ‚Äî assets protected Possible estate tax reduction Asset protection, Medicaid planning, large estates

How to Put Your Home in a Trust

  1. Create the trust document with an estate attorney ($1,500‚Äì$3,000 for a full estate plan)
  2. Transfer the deed ‚Äî a new deed is recorded with the county transferring ownership from ‚ÄúJohn Smith‚Äù to ‚ÄúJohn Smith, Trustee of the Smith Family Trust‚Äù
  3. Notify your mortgage company (most mortgages allow trust transfers without triggering due-on-sale)
  4. Update homeowners insurance to name the trust as an additional insured/interest holder
  5. Update property tax records if needed (in Idaho, this usually doesn‚Äôt trigger reassessment)

Idaho-Specific Notes

  • Idaho does NOT have a state estate tax (only federal applies, which exempts ~$13.61M per person / $27.22M per couple in 2024)
  • Idaho is a community property state ‚Äî both spouses‚Äô shares get a stepped-up basis at the first death (big capital gains advantage)
  • Idaho probate is simpler than some states, but a trust still saves time, money, and privacy
  • Small estate affidavit available for estates under $100,000 in Idaho ‚Äî but your home likely exceeds this

2. Umbrella Insurance Policy

What Is It?

An umbrella policy provides extra liability coverage above and beyond your homeowners and auto insurance. If you‚Äôre sued and the judgment exceeds your home or auto policy limits, the umbrella kicks in.

Why You Need One (Especially in Retirement)

You Have More to Lose - By retirement, you‚Äôve accumulated significant assets ‚Äî home equity, retirement accounts, savings. A lawsuit could threaten all of it. - Standard homeowners insurance typically covers $100K‚Äì$300K in liability. Auto covers $250K‚Äì$500K. A serious car accident, slip-and-fall at your property, or dog bite lawsuit can easily exceed these limits.

Lawsuits Are Expensive - Average bodily injury claim from a car accident: ~$20,000‚Äì$25,000. But serious injuries can run $500,000‚Äì$2,000,000+. - If someone is injured on your property (guest falls, pool accident, trampoline injury): $300,000‚Äì$1,000,000+ judgments are common. - If a court judgment exceeds your insurance, they come after your personal assets ‚Äî home, savings, investments, future wages.

It‚Äôs Incredibly Cheap for What You Get - $1 million umbrella policy: ~$150‚Äì$300/year - $2 million umbrella policy: ~$200‚Äì$400/year - Each additional $1M: ~$50‚Äì$100/year more - This is the best value in all of insurance. For less than $1/day, you protect your entire financial life.

What It Covers - Bodily injury liability (someone hurt on your property or in a car accident you cause) - Property damage liability - Personal liability (libel, slander, defamation claims) - Landlord liability (if you have rental properties) - Some policies cover legal defense costs on top of the coverage amount

What It Doesn‚Äôt Cover - Your own injuries or property damage - Intentional acts or criminal behavior - Business liability (need a separate business policy) - Workers‚Äô compensation claims

How Much Coverage Do You Need?

Rule of thumb: Your umbrella policy should cover at least your total net worth.

Requirements

  • Most insurers require minimum underlying coverage on your home and auto policies before issuing an umbrella:
    • Auto: typically $250K/$500K bodily injury
    • Home: typically $300K liability
  • Bundling discount: Get your umbrella from the same company as your home/auto for 10‚Äì15% savings

Where to Get It

  • State Farm, USAA, Allstate, Farmers, Liberty Mutual ‚Äî all offer umbrella policies
  • USAA (if eligible ‚Äî military/veteran connection): consistently among the best rates
  • Call your current home/auto insurer first ‚Äî bundling is cheapest
  • Cost: $150‚Äì$400/year for $1‚Äì2M coverage

3. Durable Power of Attorney ‚Äî Financial

What Is It?

A Durable Financial Power of Attorney (DPOA) is a legal document that authorizes someone you trust (your ‚Äúagent‚Äù or ‚Äúattorney-in-fact‚Äù) to make financial decisions on your behalf if you become unable to do so.

‚ÄúDurable‚Äù means it remains in effect even if you become incapacitated (mentally or physically unable to manage your affairs). A regular POA expires upon incapacity ‚Äî which is exactly when you need it most.

Why It‚Äôs Critical for Retirement

Without a DPOA, if you become incapacitated: - No one can access your bank accounts to pay bills - No one can manage your investments - No one can file your taxes - No one can sell property or access safe deposit boxes - No one can deal with insurance companies, Medicare, or Social Security on your behalf - Your family must petition a court for conservatorship/guardianship ‚Äî costs $3,000‚Äì$10,000+, takes months, requires ongoing court supervision, and is public record

With a DPOA: - Your chosen agent steps in immediately - They can pay bills, manage accounts, handle investments, file taxes, deal with insurance - No court involvement needed - Private, fast, seamless

What Powers to Include

A comprehensive financial DPOA should grant authority to:

Immediate DPOA As soon as you sign it Agent can act right away if needed; no proof of incapacity required Requires high trust ‚Äî agent has power now
Springing DPOA Only when you‚Äôre declared incapacitated No power until needed Requires doctor certification of incapacity ‚Äî can cause delays

Recommendation: Most estate attorneys recommend an immediate DPOA with someone you deeply trust. Springing POAs create bureaucratic hurdles at the worst possible time.

Important Safeguards

  • Name a successor agent in case your first choice can‚Äôt serve
  • Require accounting ‚Äî agent must keep records of all transactions
  • Limit gifting power if concerned about abuse
  • Review and update every 3‚Äì5 years ‚Äî financial institutions sometimes refuse old POAs
  • Give copies to your bank and financial institutions NOW ‚Äî many banks have their own POA forms they prefer. Getting this set up in advance prevents hassle later.

Idaho-Specific Notes

  • Idaho recognizes durable powers of attorney under Idaho Code ¬ß 15-12-101 et seq.
  • Must be signed, dated, and notarized
  • Idaho adopted the Uniform Power of Attorney Act ‚Äî provides strong protections
  • Your agent has a fiduciary duty to act in your best interest
  • Can be revoked at any time while you‚Äôre competent

4. Durable Power of Attorney ‚Äî Medical (Healthcare Directive)

What Is It?

A Durable Healthcare Power of Attorney (also called a Healthcare Proxy or part of an Advance Directive) authorizes someone you trust to make medical decisions for you if you can‚Äôt make them yourself.

This is typically combined with a Living Will into one document called an Advance Directive.

The Two Components

1. Healthcare Power of Attorney (Healthcare Proxy) - Names your agent to make medical decisions - They decide treatments, surgeries, medications, care facilities - Only activates when you can‚Äôt communicate or decide for yourself

2. Living Will - States your wishes for end-of-life care in your own words - Do you want life-sustaining treatment if terminally ill? - Do you want artificial nutrition/hydration? - Do you want CPR? - Organ donation preferences - Comfort care / pain management preferences

Why It‚Äôs Critical

Without it: - Doctors may not know your wishes - Family members may disagree about your care ‚Äî leading to conflict, court battles, and emotional trauma - A court may appoint a guardian to make your medical decisions ‚Äî someone you didn‚Äôt choose - Think of the Terri Schiavo case ‚Äî 15 years of family conflict and court battles, all preventable with a simple advance directive

With it: - Your chosen person makes decisions aligned with your values - Doctors have clear guidance - Family conflict is minimized - Your autonomy is preserved even when you can‚Äôt speak

What Your Agent Can Decide

  • Whether to pursue aggressive treatment or comfort care
  • Surgical decisions
  • Medication choices
  • Which doctors and hospitals
  • Whether to move you to a different care facility
  • Whether to discontinue life support (if consistent with your living will)
  • Access to your medical records (HIPAA authorization ‚Äî include this!)

Key Decisions to Think About Now

Have these conversations with your agent BEFORE you need them:

HIPAA Authorization

Critical addition: Include a HIPAA release form that authorizes your healthcare agent (and other family members you choose) to access your medical records. Without it, doctors may refuse to share information with your agent due to privacy laws. Many advance directive templates now include this.

Idaho-Specific Notes

  • Idaho uses the Idaho Advance Directive form ‚Äî available free from the Idaho Attorney General‚Äôs office
  • Must be signed by you and two witnesses (witnesses cannot be your healthcare agent, your doctor, or employees of your healthcare facility)
  • Notarization recommended but not strictly required in Idaho
  • Idaho recognizes POLST (Physician Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment) ‚Äî a separate medical order form completed with your doctor for those with serious illness. More specific than a living will.
  • Register your advance directive with the Idaho Secretary of State‚Äôs Living Will Registry (optional but helpful ‚Äî hospitals can access it)

5. Other Essential Documents

Last Will and Testament

  • Even with a trust, you need a pour-over will that catches any assets not transferred to the trust
  • Names a guardian for minor dependents (if applicable)
  • Names your personal representative (executor)
  • Idaho requires 2 witnesses for a valid will

Beneficiary Designations

  • These override your will and trust! Review and update:
    • 401(k) / IRA beneficiaries
    • Life insurance beneficiaries
    • Bank accounts (POD ‚Äî payable on death)
    • Brokerage accounts (TOD ‚Äî transfer on death)
  • After a divorce, job change, or death of a beneficiary ‚Äî update immediately

Letter of Intent

  • Not legally binding but incredibly helpful
  • Lists: location of important documents, account numbers, passwords, digital asset access, funeral wishes, personal messages
  • Keep with your estate documents

Digital Estate Plan

  • List all online accounts with login information
  • Password manager access (master password to a trusted person)
  • Social media wishes (memorialize, delete, etc.)
  • Cryptocurrency access (seed phrases, wallet info)
